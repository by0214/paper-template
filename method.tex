\section{Method}

We seek to learn step-level process supervision from reasoning trajectories that
only come with final outcome rewards.
The core difficulty is that the observed reward is defined on the full
trajectory rather than on individual steps.
Our solution is to construct two complementary supervision signals from the
trajectory data itself.
The first is the \emph{contextual contribution} of a step, \ie whether that
step helps or hurts the predicted outcome of the particular trajectory in which
it appears.
The second is the \emph{intrinsic value} of a reasoning pattern, \ie whether
that kind of step tends to correlate with successful reasoning across
trajectories.
These two views capture complementary aspects of process supervision:
one is trajectory-specific and causally grounded, while the other is
cross-trajectory and distributional.
SPRM distills both into a unified process reward model (PRM).

Figure~\ref{fig:sprm_overview} summarizes the full pipeline.

We first learn a differentiable trajectory scorer from outcome-only rewards.
This scorer turns sparse final outcomes into a trajectory value function on
which contextual step credit can be defined.
We then construct a local signal for trajectory-specific contribution and a
global signal for cross-trajectory pattern value, and finally distill both into
a dual-head PRM.

\subsection{Local Step Credit from Outcome-Only Trajectories}
\label{sec:method_local}

We begin from a cooperative-game view of step credit assignment.
Consider a dataset
\(
\mathcal{D}=\{(\tau_i,r_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}
\),
where
\(
\tau_i=(q_i,s_{i,1},\ldots,s_{i,T_i})
\)
is a reasoning trajectory and \(r_i\in\{0,1\}\) is its final reward.
For each trajectory, the reasoning steps are treated as players
\(
\mathcal{N}_i=\{1,\ldots,T_i\}
\),
and a coalition \(S\subseteq\mathcal{N}_i\) is assigned value \(v(S)\).
Under the classical discrete Shapley formulation, the credit of step \(t\) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:discrete_shapley}
\phi_{i,t}^{\mathrm{sha}}
=
\frac{1}{T_i!}
\sum_{\pi\in\Pi(\mathcal{N}_i)}
\Big[
v\!\big(\mathrm{Pre}_{\pi}(t)\cup\{t\}\big)
-
v\!\big(\mathrm{Pre}_{\pi}(t)\big)
\Big],
\end{equation}
where \(\Pi(\mathcal{N}_i)\) is the set of all permutations of
\(\mathcal{N}_i\), and \(\mathrm{Pre}_{\pi}(t)\) denotes the predecessors of
step \(t\) under permutation \(\pi\).
This quantity measures the expected marginal contribution of a step over all
possible insertion orders \citep{shapley1953value,pmlr-v119-sundararajan20b}.

Reasoning trajectories, however, are temporally ordered and causally
constrained, so arbitrary permutations are not admissible.
Let
\(
P_{i,t}=\{1,\ldots,t\}
\)
for \(t\in\{0,\ldots,T_i\}\), with \(P_{i,0}=\emptyset\).

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:ordered_shapley_degenerate}
If only the observed causal order
\(
1\prec 2\prec \cdots \prec T_i
\)
is admissible, then the discrete Shapley value in
\Cref{eq:discrete_shapley} degenerates to
\begin{equation}
\phi_{i,t}^{\mathrm{ord}}
=
v(P_{i,t})-v(P_{i,t-1}).
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\textit{Proof sketch.}
Under a strict order, \(\mathrm{Pre}_{\pi}(t)\) degenerates to
\(P_{i,t-1}\).
Substituting this into \Cref{eq:discrete_shapley} directly yields the value
difference above.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:reward_shaping_invariance}
Let \(v^\star(P_{i,t})\) denote the optimal trajectory value on prefix state
\(P_{i,t}\), and define the dense step reward
\(
\tilde r_{i,t}^\star
=
v^\star(P_{i,t})-v^\star(P_{i,t-1}).
\)
Then a PRM trained on this value-difference reward induces the same optimal
policy as the original sparse terminal reward.
\end{lemma}

Formal proof of \Cref{lem:reward_shaping_invariance} is deferred to
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_local_credit_proofs}.
The key observation is that \(\tilde r_{i,t}^\star\) is a potential-based
reward shaping term over prefix states and therefore preserves the optimal
policy under the reward-transformation theorem of
\citep{ng1999policy}.

These two lemmas explain why classical discrete Shapley is insufficient in our
setting: once order is fixed, it collapses to a prefix-wise value difference
that mostly redistributes the same sparse outcome reward.

To obtain a more useful local signal, we encode each trajectory into a matrix
\(
X_i=[x_{i,0},x_{i,1},\ldots,x_{i,T_i}]
\in\mathbb{R}^{(T_i+1)\times d},
\)
where \(x_{i,0}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) is the \textit{question\_end} representation and
\(x_{i,t}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) is the representation of step
\(s_{i,t}\).
These representations are produced by an encoder with sufficient semantic
capacity; in our implementation, this encoder is the backbone LLM.

We then learn a lightweight trajectory scorer
\(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}:\mathbb{R}^{(T_i+1)\times d}\rightarrow[0,1]\)
from scratch on \(\mathcal{D}\), and interpret it as a learnable
approximation to \(v\).
This scorer maps step representations to a smooth trajectory-level value signal
that supports credit assignment, rather than serving as an external judge.
It can be trained directly from the step representations and the final reward:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage1_objective}
\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star
=
\arg\min_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}
\mathbb{E}_{(\tau,r)\sim\mathcal{D}}
\left[
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}(\mathrm{encoder}(\tau)),r\right)
\right].
\end{equation}
where \(\mathrm{BCE}(\cdot,\cdot)\) denotes the binary cross-entropy loss with
binary target \(r\in\{0,1\}\).

After training, \(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}\) serves as a
differentiable trajectory value model.
Rather than using the discrete difference from
\Cref{lem:ordered_shapley_degenerate} directly, we define local marginal credit
through Aumann--Shapley attribution.
Intuitively, this attribution measures the marginal contribution of a step by
integrating the scorer gradient along the straight-line path from a baseline
representation to the observed step representation, thereby producing
continuous credit without relying on external judges and their associated
biases.
To instantiate this path, let \(b\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) be a semantically
uninformative baseline vector, and let \(B_i=[b,\ldots,b]\) be the
corresponding baseline sequence with the same shape as \(X_i\).
The interpolation path from baseline to observation is
\(
\gamma_i(\alpha)=B_i+\alpha(X_i-B_i)
\)
for \(\alpha\in[0,1]\).
Using Aumann--Shapley attribution implemented by integrated gradients
\citep{sundararajan2017axiomatic}, we define the local supervision signal for
step \(s_{i,t}\) as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_local}
\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}
=
\left(x_{i,t}-b\right)^\top
\int_0^1
\nabla_{x_{i,t}}
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}\!\left(\gamma_i(\alpha)\right)
\,d\alpha,
\qquad
t\in\{1,\ldots,T_i\}.
\end{equation}

This quantity measures whether the current step increases or decreases the
predicted value of the full trajectory when evaluated in its observed context,
without collapsing the signal to a single discrete prefix difference.

Integrated gradients measure the marginal effect of a step on the scorer
output.
To additionally capture interactions between steps, we complement them with a
pairwise counterfactual check.
Let \(X_i^{(-t)}\) be the sequence obtained by replacing only \(x_{i,t}\) with
the baseline vector \(b\), and let \(X_i^{(-t,-u)}\) replace both \(x_{i,t}\)
and \(x_{i,u}\) with \(b\).
The direct counterfactual effect of step \(t\) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:direct_counterfactual}
R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{dir}}
=
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i)
-
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t)}).
\end{equation}
To account for step interactions while keeping the perturbation computation
tractable, we further define a pairwise interaction term over an ordered local
window.
Although the explicit perturbation is local in index space, each step
representation already summarizes its causal prefix, so these windowed
interactions can still reflect dependencies that propagate through long
reasoning chains:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_window}
\mathcal{W}(t)
=
\{u \mid 1 \le u < t,\; t-u \le w\},
\end{equation}
where \(w\) is a small window size.
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_interaction}
R_{i,t,u}^{\mathrm{int}}
=
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i)
-
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t)})
-
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-u)})
+
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t,-u)}),
\end{equation}
We then construct deterministic interaction weights
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_weight}
\bar g_{i,t,u}
=
\frac{\mathbf{1}(u\in\mathcal{W}(t))\max(R_{i,t,u}^{\mathrm{int}},0)}
{\sum_{v\in\mathcal{W}(t)} \max(R_{i,t,v}^{\mathrm{int}},0)+\epsilon},
\end{equation}
and define the interaction-enhanced causal score
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_counterfactual}
R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}
=
R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{dir}}
+
\beta\sum_{u\in\mathcal{W}(t)} \bar g_{i,t,u} R_{i,t,u}^{\mathrm{int}},
\end{equation}
where \(\beta\) controls the strength of the pairwise correction.
In our framework, \(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\) is not used as the main local
target.
Its role is to provide a discrete consistency check for the sign of
\(\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}\).

\subsection{Global Semantic Prior}
\label{sec:method_global}

The global branch targets a different quantity: the \emph{intrinsic value} of a
reasoning pattern across trajectories, namely whether that pattern tends to
correlate with successful reasoning beyond a single rollout.
A step that is correct in itself can still appear inside an unsuccessful
trajectory, for example when it follows a mistaken earlier conclusion and is
therefore embedded in an incorrect reasoning chain.
The goal of the global branch is to estimate this intrinsic step value across
contexts, so that the reward signal becomes less sensitive to trajectory-local
contextual variation and therefore more stable.
For this reason, the global branch does not follow the same ordered credit
logic as the local branch.

We operationalize this idea with a semantic neighborhood prior.
For each step representation \(x_{i,t}\), we retrieve its top-\(M\) nearest
neighbors from other trajectories and denote the resulting set by
\(\mathcal{H}_M(i,t)\).
Let \(\omega_{i,t,j,\ell}\ge 0\) be the similarity-based weight assigned to
neighbor \((j,\ell)\in\mathcal{H}_M(i,t)\).
The neighborhood success estimate is
\[
\hat r_{i,t}
=
\frac{
\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{H}_M(i,t)} \omega_{i,t,j,\ell} r_j
}{
\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{H}_M(i,t)} \omega_{i,t,j,\ell}
}.
\]
Comparing this quantity with the dataset-wide average reward
\(
\bar r=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} r_i
\),
we obtain the global supervision signal
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_global}
\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}
=
\hat r_{i,t}-\bar r.
\end{equation}
This quantity measures whether the semantic neighborhood of \(x_{i,t}\) is more
or less associated with success than the dataset average, and provides a stable
complement to the trajectory-specific local signal.

\subsection{Training the PRM and Inference}
\label{sec:method_prm}

The local and global signals above are used to supervise the final PRM at the
positions corresponding to reasoning steps.
The PRM reads the same boundary-augmented sequence and uses a shared backbone
with two task-specific heads, jointly parameterized by
\(\theta_{\mathrm{PRM}}\).
For step \(s_{i,t}\), the two heads predict
\(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}\in(0,1)\) and
\(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}\in(0,1)\).
We convert the constructed signals into binary targets
\[
y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}=\mathbf{1}\!\left(\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}>0\right),
\qquad
y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}=\mathbf{1}\!\left(\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}>0\right).
\]

For the local head, we only trust supervision when the continuous attribution
and the discrete counterfactual check agree on the sign of the step effect.
We therefore define the consistency mask
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:local_mask}
m_{i,t}
=
\mathbf{1}\!\left(
\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}} \cdot R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}} > 0
\right).
\end{equation}
The PRM objective is
\begin{align}
\label{eq:stage3_objective}
\theta_{\mathrm{PRM}}^\star
&=
\arg\min_{\theta_{\mathrm{PRM}}}
\mathbb{E}_{(\tau_i,r_i)\sim\mathcal{D}}
\Big[
\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}
m_{i,t}\,
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}, y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}\right)
\notag\\
&\quad+
\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}, y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}\right)
\Big].
\end{align}
where the local branch captures trajectory-specific contextual credit and the
global branch injects a cross-trajectory semantic prior.
Because the two heads are trained jointly with a shared backbone, we do not
introduce an explicit mixing weight in the training loss.

At inference time, we combine the two heads as
\[
\hat y_{i,t}
=
\lambda \hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}
+
(1-\lambda)\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}},
\]
where \(\lambda\in[0,1]\) controls the trade-off between the local and global
heads at inference time.
The resulting score balances local causal evidence with global semantic
regularity and serves as the final step-level reward.
