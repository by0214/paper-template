\section{Method}

We aim to learn step-level process supervision from reasoning trajectories that
only come with final outcome rewards.
Formally, let
\(
\mathcal{D}=\{(\tau_i,r_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}
\)
be a dataset of trajectories, where
\(
\tau_i=(q_i,s_{i,1},\ldots,s_{i,T_i})
\)
contains a question \(q_i\), a sequence of reasoning steps
\(s_{i,1},\ldots,s_{i,T_i}\), and a final reward \(r_i\in[0,1]\).
Our goal is to transform this sparse trajectory-level signal into trainable
step-level supervision for process reward modeling, without requiring external
step annotations, per-step Monte Carlo rollouts, or judge models at labeling
time.

The central design question is how to assess the quality of an individual
reasoning step when only the final outcome is observed.
In our view, step quality has two complementary aspects.
One is contextual: a step should be judged by the role it plays in the
specific trajectory where it appears.
The other is intrinsic: beyond any single context, a step also reflects a
reasoning pattern whose value may be estimated from how similar patterns
behave across many trajectories.
This view motivates us to construct two supervision signals, a local one and a
global one, and then distill them into a unified process reward model.
To make this view operational, we represent the question and each reasoning
step using hidden states from the LLM backbone.
Let \(x_{i,t}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) denote the \(d\)-dimensional representation of
the \(t\)-th segment in trajectory \(i\), where \(t=0\) refers to the question
segment and \(t\in\{1,\ldots,T_i\}\) refers to reasoning steps.
We write the full trajectory representation as
\(
X_i=[x_{i,0},x_{i,1},\ldots,x_{i,T_i}]
\),
and use \(x_{i,t,j}\) to denote the \(j\)-th dimension of \(x_{i,t}\).
Implementation details of the encoder are deferred to the appendix.

\subsection{Constructing Step-Level Supervision}
\label{stage2}

We begin by constructing the two supervision signals that instantiate the
above view of step quality.
The local signal asks how much the current step contributes to the predicted
outcome of the trajectory in which it appears.
The global signal asks whether the underlying reasoning pattern tends to be
useful across the dataset.
Both are inspired by the Shapley view of credit assignment, but they operate
at different levels: the local signal uses differentiable step-level
attribution, while the global signal estimates the marginal value of semantic
patterns from cross-trajectory statistics.

\subsubsection{Learning a Trajectory-Level Value Proxy for Local Attribution}
\label{stage1}

The local signal requires a scorer that can evaluate a step in the context of
the entire reasoning chain.
Because the observed reward is only defined for a complete trajectory, we
first learn a differentiable scorer
\(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}\) over full trajectories.
Its role is to provide a global view of the reasoning process and capture how
different steps jointly affect the final result, so that local attribution is
performed against the full context rather than isolated prefixes or heuristics.

Given a trajectory representation \(X_i\), the scorer predicts a final value
\(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}(X_i)\in[0,1]\).
We train it from trajectory-level supervision by solving
% \begin{equation}
% \label{eq:stage1_objective}
% \theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star
% =
% \arg\min_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}
% \sum_{i=1}^{N}
% \ell\!\left(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}(X_i),r_i\right),
% \end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage1_objective}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{scorer}}(\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}})
=
\mathbb{E}_{(\tau,r)\sim\mathcal{D}}
\left[
\ell\!\left(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}(X),r\right)
\right]
\end{equation}

where \(\ell(\cdot,\cdot)\) is the binary cross-entropy loss.
After training,
\(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}\) provides the differentiable value proxy
used to model local step contribution.

\subsubsection{Local Aumann--Shapley Attribution}

To quantify the contextual value of a step, we adopt a continuous Shapley view
and ask how the predicted final value changes as the trajectory moves from a
neutral reference state to the observed one.
For differentiable models, this corresponds to Aumann--Shapley attribution,
implemented via integrated gradients.
A key ingredient in this construction is a baseline representation: a
semantically uninformative reference that should not systematically bias the
prediction toward success or failure.
In our setting, let \(b\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) be such a baseline representation and define
\(X_i^{\mathrm{base}}=[b,b,\ldots,b]\).
This baseline removes the semantic content of all segments, including the
question segment, so that attribution is computed with respect to the full
reasoning context.
We then consider the interpolation path
\(
\gamma_i(\alpha)=X_i^{\mathrm{base}}+\alpha(X_i-X_i^{\mathrm{base}})
\)
for \(\alpha\in[0,1]\).
Accumulating the marginal effect of the current step along this path yields a
path-integrated attribution.
For readability, we write the resulting signal as a function of the step
\(s_{i,t}\), although it is computed from its representation \(x_{i,t}\).
The local signal assigned to step \(s_{i,t}\) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_local}
\delta_{\mathrm{local}}(s_{i,t})
=
\sum_{j=1}^{d}
\left(x_{i,t,j}-b_j\right)
\int_0^1
\frac{\partial F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(\gamma_i(\alpha))}
{\partial x_{i,t,j}}
\,d\alpha.
\end{equation}
Intuitively, \(\delta_{\mathrm{local}}(s_{i,t})\) measures whether the current
step increases or decreases the predicted final reward when evaluated as part
of the complete reasoning trajectory.

\subsubsection{Counterfactual Consistency Check}

Although Aumann--Shapley attribution provides a principled continuous
decomposition of local credit, it is still computed at the representation
dimension level.
As a result, the attribution mass can be dispersed across many dimensions,
which may blur step-level causal structure or become unstable along the
integration path.
To recover a more explicit step-level causal view, we introduce a discrete
counterfactual ablation score and use it as an auxiliary consistency signal.
Let \(X_i^{(-t)}\) denote the trajectory obtained by replacing only the current
step representation \(x_{i,t}\) with the baseline vector \(b\), while keeping
all other segments unchanged.
We define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:r_causal}
R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}
=
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i)-F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t)}).
\end{equation}
If \(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}>0\), removing the current step lowers the
predicted final value, which suggests that the step is supportive for the
outcome.
In our framework, this counterfactual quantity is not used as a direct
training label; instead, it is used later to filter local supervision targets
that are inconsistent with the corresponding discrete perturbation.

\subsubsection{Global Semantic Prior}

Local attribution captures how a step functions inside one particular
trajectory, but step quality should not be determined by a single context
alone.
In long reasoning chains, the contribution of a step may fluctuate
substantially with its surrounding context, even when it instantiates a
generally helpful reasoning pattern.
To capture this more stable aspect of step quality, we complement local credit
with a global semantic prior estimated directly from the dataset.

Here the object of attribution shifts from individual steps to recurring
reasoning patterns.
These patterns are not specified manually.
Instead, each pattern is induced by a neighborhood of semantically similar
steps in the representation space: the k-nearest-neighbor (KNN) neighborhood of a step acts as a
soft cluster that captures the shared reasoning behavior of those steps.
From a Shapley perspective, one may view each semantic pattern as a player in
a cooperative game whose payoff is final trajectory success.
Its ideal value would be its average marginal contribution across different
co-occurrence contexts.
In practice, exact discrete Shapley computation over all pattern subsets is
intractable at dataset scale.
We therefore adopt a practical approximation that keeps only the main effect
of a pattern relative to a global baseline.

Concretely, for each step \(s_{i,t}\), we retrieve the top-\(M\) semantically
nearest steps from other trajectories and use this neighborhood to estimate
how successful the corresponding pattern tends to be.
Let \(\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)\) denote this neighbor set, and let
\(w_{j,\ell}\ge 0\) be the similarity-based weight assigned to neighbor
\((j,\ell)\).
The resulting neighborhood success estimate is
\[
\hat r(s_{i,t})
=
\frac{
\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)} w_{j,\ell}r_j
}{
\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)} w_{j,\ell}
}.
\]
Comparing this quantity with the dataset-wide average reward
\(
\bar r=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}r_i
\),
we obtain the global prior
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_global}
\delta_{\mathrm{global}}(s_{i,t})
=
\hat r(s_{i,t})-\bar r.
\end{equation}
A positive value indicates that semantically similar steps tend to occur in
successful trajectories more often than average, while a negative value
suggests the opposite.
Although this quantity is only a practical approximation rather than an exact
Shapley value, it plays the role of a distribution-level marginal effect and
provides a stable complement to the local causal signal.

\subsection{Distilling Supervision into the Process Reward Model}
\label{stage3}

The two signals above are then used to supervise the final process reward
model at step boundaries.
For each trajectory, we insert a special boundary token
\(\langle\texttt{ST\_END}\rangle\) after the question and after every reasoning
step.
The model reads the entire sequence and outputs one reward prediction at each
boundary position for the immediately preceding segment.

We use a shared backbone with two task-specific heads, jointly parameterized by
\(\theta_{\mathrm{LLM}}\).
At the boundary corresponding to step \(s_{i,t}\), the model predicts
\(\hat\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{local}}\) and
\(\hat\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{global}}\), which are trained against binarized
targets derived from the two constructed signals,
\(
\bar{\delta}_{i,t}^{\mathrm{local}}=\mathbf{1}(\delta_{\mathrm{local}}(s_{i,t})>0)
\)
and
\(
\bar{\delta}_{i,t}^{\mathrm{global}}=\mathbf{1}(\delta_{\mathrm{global}}(s_{i,t})>0)
\).

For the local head, we further require consistency between the continuous
attribution and the discrete counterfactual check.
Specifically, we define a mask
\(
m_{i,t}
=
\mathbf{1}\!\left(
\delta_{\mathrm{local}}(s_{i,t})\cdot R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}>0
\right),
\)
and skip the local loss whenever \(m_{i,t}=0\).
In this way, local supervision is used only when both views agree on the
direction of the step's effect.

The training objective is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage3_objective}
\begin{split}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PRM}}(\theta_{\mathrm{LLM}})
=
\mathbb{E}_{(\tau,r)\sim\mathcal{D}}
\Big[
&\lambda_{\mathrm{local}}
\sum_t
m_t\,
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(
\hat{\delta}_t^{\mathrm{local}},
\bar{\delta}_t^{\mathrm{local}}
\right) \\
&+
\lambda_{\mathrm{global}}
\sum_t
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(
\hat{\delta}_t^{\mathrm{global}},
\bar{\delta}_t^{\mathrm{global}}
\right)
\Big]
\end{split}
\end{equation}
The local head encourages the model to capture trajectory-specific causal
credit, while the global head injects a more stable semantic prior gathered
from the data distribution.

At inference time, we combine the two heads as
\(
\hat\delta_{i,t}
=
\lambda_{\mathrm{local}}\hat\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{local}}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{global}}\hat\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{global}}.
\)
The resulting score balances local causal evidence and global semantic
regularity, and can be used as the final step-level process reward.
