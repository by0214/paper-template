\section{Method}

We seek to learn step-level process supervision from reasoning trajectories that
only come with final outcome rewards.
The core difficulty is that the observed reward is defined on the full
trajectory rather than on individual steps.
Our solution is to construct two complementary supervision signals from the
trajectory data itself.
The local signal estimates the \emph{contextual contribution} of a step within
its current trajectory, while the global signal estimates the \emph{intrinsic
value} of the underlying reasoning pattern across trajectories.
These signals are then distilled into a unified process reward model (PRM).

Figure~\ref{fig:sprm_overview} summarizes the full pipeline.
We consider a dataset
\(
\mathcal{D}=\{(\tau_i,r_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}
\),
where
\(
\tau_i=(q_i,s_{i,1},\ldots,s_{i,T_i})
\)
is a reasoning trajectory and \(r_i\in[0,1]\) is its final outcome reward.
To represent trajectories, we insert a special boundary token
\(\langle\texttt{ST\_END}\rangle\) after the question and after every reasoning
step, and use the final-layer hidden state at each boundary as the
representation of the immediately preceding segment.
Let \(x_{i,0}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) denote the question-boundary representation and
\(x_{i,t}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) denote the representation of step \(s_{i,t}\) for
\(t\in\{1,\ldots,T_i\}\).
We write the full boundary sequence as
\(
X_i=[x_{i,0},x_{i,1},\ldots,x_{i,T_i}]
\).
The question boundary is used as a contextual anchor in trajectory scoring and
credit assignment, while supervision targets are constructed for reasoning
steps \(t\ge 1\).

\subsection{Framework Overview}

SPRM follows a three-stage pipeline.
First, we learn a trajectory-level scorer that predicts the final outcome
reward of the full reasoning chain.
Its purpose is not merely to support a later attribution formula; rather, it
turns sparse outcome supervision into a differentiable trajectory value
function on which local credit can be defined.
Second, we construct two step-level supervision signals from this shared
representation space.
The local signal asks whether the current step helps or hurts the current
trajectory, while the global signal asks whether the underlying reasoning
pattern tends to be useful across trajectories.
Third, these two signals are distilled into a dual-head PRM so that the final
reward model inherits both trajectory-specific credit and cross-trajectory
stability.

\subsection{Local Step Credit from Outcome-Only Trajectories}
\label{sec:method_local}

The local branch targets the \emph{contextual contribution} of a reasoning
step.
Because steps in a reasoning chain are temporally ordered and causally
dependent, this quantity should respect the observed order rather than average
over arbitrary permutations.
In this sense, the local problem is closer to ordered marginal credit than to
classical Shapley values over exchangeable players.
At the same time, a single ordered discrete difference is too brittle to be
used directly as supervision, because it depends on one hard perturbation of a
learned value estimator.
We therefore first learn a differentiable trajectory value function and then
define continuous local credit on top of it, with an additional discrete
counterfactual check for reliability.

The first obstacle is that the observed reward is available only at the
trajectory level.
Before assigning credit to any individual step, we therefore need a model that
maps the full trajectory representation \(X_i\) to a scalar estimate of its
outcome.
We learn a trajectory-level scorer
\(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}:\mathbb{R}^{(T_i+1)\times d}\rightarrow[0,1]\),
where \(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}(X_i)\) is interpreted as the predicted
success value of the whole trajectory.
This scorer is the value function on which local credit assignment is defined.
Its parameters are learned from the same trajectory-level rewards available in
the dataset:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage1_objective}
\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star
=
\arg\min_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}
\mathbb{E}_{(\tau,r)\sim\mathcal{D}}
\left[
\ell\!\left(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}(X),r\right)
\right],
\end{equation}
where \(\ell(\cdot,\cdot)\) is the binary cross-entropy loss with a possibly
soft target \(r\in[0,1]\).
After training, \(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}\) provides a differentiable
trajectory value estimate that captures how the full reasoning chain supports
the final outcome.

We then use this value function to define continuous local credit.
Let \(b\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) be a semantically uninformative baseline vector, and
let \(B_i=[b,\ldots,b]\) be the corresponding baseline sequence with the same
shape as \(X_i\).
The interpolation path from baseline to observation is
\(
\gamma_i(\alpha)=B_i+\alpha(X_i-B_i)
\)
for \(\alpha\in[0,1]\).
Using Aumann--Shapley attribution implemented by integrated gradients, we
define the local supervision signal for step \(s_{i,t}\) as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_local}
\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}
=
\left(x_{i,t}-b\right)^\top
\int_0^1
\nabla_{x_{i,t}}
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}\!\left(\gamma_i(\alpha)\right)
\,d\alpha,
\qquad
t\in\{1,\ldots,T_i\}.
\end{equation}
This quantity measures whether the current step increases or decreases the
predicted value of the full trajectory when evaluated in its observed context.

Integrated gradients provide a smooth estimate of ordered local credit, but the
signal still depends on a learned value function and can therefore be noisy.
We complement it with a discrete pairwise counterfactual check.
Let \(X_i^{(-t)}\) be the sequence obtained by replacing only \(x_{i,t}\) with
the baseline vector \(b\), and let \(X_i^{(-t,-u)}\) replace both \(x_{i,t}\)
and \(x_{i,u}\) with \(b\).
The direct counterfactual effect of step \(t\) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:direct_counterfactual}
R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{dir}}
=
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i)
-
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t)}).
\end{equation}
To model short-range dependencies between adjacent reasoning steps, we further
compute a pairwise interaction term
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_interaction}
I_{i,t,u}
=
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i)
-
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t)})
-
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-u)})
+
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t,-u)}),
\end{equation}
but only within an ordered local window
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_window}
\mathcal{W}(t)
=
\{u \mid 1 \le u < t,\; t-u \le w\},
\end{equation}
where \(w\) is a small window size.
We then construct deterministic interaction weights
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_weight}
\bar g_{i,t,u}
=
\frac{\mathbf{1}(u\in\mathcal{W}(t))\max(I_{i,t,u},0)}
{\sum_{v\in\mathcal{W}(t)} \max(I_{i,t,v},0)+\epsilon},
\end{equation}
and define the interaction-enhanced counterfactual score
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_counterfactual}
\tilde R_{i,t}
=
R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{dir}}
+
\beta\sum_{u\in\mathcal{W}(t)} \bar g_{i,t,u} I_{i,t,u},
\end{equation}
where \(\beta\) controls the strength of the pairwise correction.
In our framework, \(\tilde R_{i,t}\) is not used as the main local target.
Its role is to provide a discrete consistency check for the sign of
\(\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}\).

\subsection{Global Semantic Prior}
\label{sec:method_global}

The global branch targets a different quantity: the \emph{intrinsic value} of a
reasoning pattern across trajectories.
A step can be locally helpful inside one trajectory yet instantiate a pattern
that is globally unreliable, and the reverse can also happen.
For this reason, the global branch does not follow the same ordered credit
logic as the local branch.
From a Shapley perspective, it is closer to retaining the main effect of a
semantic pattern relative to a dataset-level baseline.

We operationalize this idea with a semantic neighborhood prior.
For each step representation \(x_{i,t}\), we retrieve its top-\(M\) nearest
neighbors from other trajectories and denote the resulting set by
\(\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)\).
Let \(\omega_{i,t,j,\ell}\ge 0\) be the similarity-based weight assigned to
neighbor \((j,\ell)\in\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)\).
The neighborhood success estimate is
\[
\hat r_{i,t}
=
\frac{
\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)} \omega_{i,t,j,\ell} r_j
}{
\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)} \omega_{i,t,j,\ell}
}.
\]
Comparing this quantity with the dataset-wide average reward
\(
\bar r=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} r_i
\),
we obtain the global supervision signal
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_global}
\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}
=
\hat r_{i,t}-\bar r.
\end{equation}
This quantity is a practical approximation to the marginal main effect of the
reasoning pattern represented by \(x_{i,t}\), and provides a stable complement
to the trajectory-specific local signal.

\subsection{Training the PRM and Inference}
\label{sec:method_prm}

The local and global signals above are used to supervise the final PRM at the
boundary positions corresponding to reasoning steps.
The PRM reads the same boundary-augmented sequence and uses a shared backbone
with two task-specific heads, jointly parameterized by
\(\theta_{\mathrm{PRM}}\).
For step \(s_{i,t}\), the two heads predict
\(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}\in(0,1)\) and
\(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}\in(0,1)\).
We convert the constructed signals into binary targets
\[
y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}=\mathbf{1}\!\left(\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}>0\right),
\qquad
y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}=\mathbf{1}\!\left(\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}>0\right).
\]

For the local head, we only trust supervision when the continuous attribution
and the discrete counterfactual check agree on the sign of the step effect.
We therefore define the consistency mask
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:local_mask}
m_{i,t}
=
\mathbf{1}\!\left(
\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}} \cdot \tilde R_{i,t} > 0
\right).
\end{equation}
The PRM objective is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage3_objective}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PRM}}(\theta_{\mathrm{PRM}})
=
\mathbb{E}_{(\tau_i,r_i)\sim\mathcal{D}}
\Big[
&\lambda_{\mathrm{loc}}
\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}
m_{i,t}\,
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}, y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}\right) \\
&+
\lambda_{\mathrm{glo}}
\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}, y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}\right)
\Big],
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where the local branch captures trajectory-specific contextual credit and the
global branch injects a cross-trajectory semantic prior.

At inference time, we combine the two heads as
\[
\hat y_{i,t}
=
\eta_{\mathrm{loc}} \hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}
+
\eta_{\mathrm{glo}} \hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}},
\]
where \(\eta_{\mathrm{loc}}\) and \(\eta_{\mathrm{glo}}\) control the
contribution of the two heads.
The resulting score balances local causal evidence with global semantic
regularity and serves as the final step-level reward.
