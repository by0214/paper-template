\section{Method}

We aim to learn step-level process supervision from reasoning trajectories that
only come with final outcome rewards.
Formally, let
\(
\mathcal{D}=\{(\tau_i,r_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}
\)
be a dataset of trajectories, where
\(
\tau_i=(q_i,s_{i,1},\ldots,s_{i,T_i})
\)
contains a question \(q_i\) and a sequence of reasoning steps
\(s_{i,1},\ldots,s_{i,T_i}\), and \(r_i\in[0,1]\) is the final reward of the
whole trajectory.
Our goal is to transform this sparse trajectory-level signal into trainable
step-level supervision for process reward modeling, without requiring external
step annotations, per-step Monte Carlo rollouts, or judge models at labeling
time.

To do so, we proceed in three stages.
We first learn a differentiable scorer over complete trajectories, so that the
effect of each step can be evaluated in the context of the full reasoning
chain.
We then use this scorer to derive an instance-level local credit signal, while
separately mining a distribution-level global prior directly from the dataset.
Finally, we distill both signals into a reward model that predicts step-level
quality at reasoning boundaries.

We encode the question and each reasoning step with the LLM backbone and use
their hidden representations as step features.
Let \(x_{i,t}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) denote the \(d\)-dimensional representation of
the \(t\)-th segment in trajectory \(i\), where \(t=0\) refers to the question
segment and \(t\in\{1,\ldots,T_i\}\) refers to reasoning steps.
We write the full trajectory representation as
\(
X_i=[x_{i,0},x_{i,1},\ldots,x_{i,T_i}]
\),
and use \(x_{i,t,j}\) to denote the \(j\)-th dimension of \(x_{i,t}\).
For readability, the supervision signals in this section are written as
functions of the step \(s_{i,t}\), although they are computed from its
representation \(x_{i,t}\).
Implementation details of the encoder are deferred to the appendix.

\subsection{Stage 1: Learning a Trajectory-Level Value Proxy}
\label{stage1}

The final reward tells us whether a complete trajectory succeeds, but it does
not directly reveal which intermediate steps were responsible for that outcome.
To recover such step-level credit, we first learn a differentiable scorer
\(F_{\theta_1}\) over complete trajectories.
The purpose of this scorer is to view the entire reasoning chain at once and
capture how different steps jointly affect the final result, so that later
attribution is performed in full context rather than by isolated heuristics.

Given a trajectory representation \(X_i\), the scorer predicts a final value
\(F_{\theta_1}(X_i)\in[0,1]\).
We train it from the trajectory-level supervision by solving
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage1_objective}
\theta_1^\star
=
\arg\min_{\theta_1}
\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\ell\!\left(F_{\theta_1}(X_i),r_i\right),
\end{equation}
where \(\ell(\cdot,\cdot)\) is the binary cross-entropy loss.
After training, \(F_{\theta_1^\star}\) serves as a differentiable proxy of
final trajectory quality and will be used to estimate how each step changes
the predicted outcome.

\subsection{Stage 2: Constructing Step-Level Supervision}
\label{stage2}

Once we have a differentiable scorer for complete trajectories, the next
question is how to judge the quality of an individual reasoning step.
A good step should first be useful in the concrete trajectory where it
appears: if removing or weakening this step changes the predicted final
outcome, then the step carries meaningful local credit.
At the same time, step quality should not be determined only by one specific
context.
In long reasoning chains, the value of a step may fluctuate substantially with
the surrounding trajectory, even when the step itself expresses a generally
helpful reasoning pattern.
To assess this more intrinsic aspect of step quality, we also need a
distribution-level view that asks whether semantically similar steps tend to
appear in successful trajectories.

These two perspectives lead to two complementary supervision signals.
The first is a local signal that measures the step's contribution within a
specific trajectory.
The second is a global signal that measures the historical usefulness of the
underlying reasoning pattern across the dataset.
Both are inspired by the Shapley view of credit assignment, but they operate
at different levels.
The local signal adopts a differentiable step-level attribution mechanism,
where the path-integrated credit corresponds to Aumann--Shapley attribution.
The global signal treats recurrent semantic patterns as players in a
distribution-level cooperative game, and approximates their marginal value by
cross-trajectory statistics.

\paragraph{Local Causal Attribution}

We begin with the local view.
For a given trajectory, we would like to know how much the current step
contributes to the predicted final success when evaluated in the context of
the whole reasoning process.
To make this idea concrete, we compare the observed trajectory with a baseline
trajectory that removes the semantic content of all segments.
Let \(b\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) be a baseline representation and define
\(X_i^{\mathrm{base}}=[b,b,\ldots,b]\).
This means that both the question segment and all reasoning steps participate
in the attribution path, so the resulting credit is computed with respect to
the full reasoning context.

We then consider the interpolation path
\(
\gamma_i(\alpha)=X_i^{\mathrm{base}}+\alpha(X_i-X_i^{\mathrm{base}})
\)
for \(\alpha\in[0,1]\), and accumulate how sensitive the predicted final value
is to each feature dimension of the current step along this path.
This yields a path-integrated attribution, which corresponds to integrated
gradients, or equivalently, Aumann--Shapley attribution in the differentiable
setting.
The resulting local signal for step \(s_{i,t}\) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_local}
\delta_{\mathrm{local}}(s_{i,t})
=
\sum_{j=1}^{d}
\left(x_{i,t,j}-b_j\right)
\int_0^1
\frac{\partial F_{\theta_1^\star}(\gamma_i(\alpha))}
{\partial x_{i,t,j}}
\,d\alpha.
\end{equation}
Intuitively, \(\delta_{\mathrm{local}}(s_{i,t})\) measures whether introducing
the current step raises or lowers the predicted final reward when the entire
trajectory is taken into account.

However, a purely continuous attribution can still be noisy.
Because the signal is distributed over high-dimensional representations, it
may blur semantically important reasoning steps.
We therefore introduce a discrete counterfactual check.
Let \(X_i^{(-t)}\) denote the trajectory obtained by replacing only the
current step representation \(x_{i,t}\) with the baseline vector \(b\), while
keeping the rest of the trajectory unchanged.
We define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:r_causal}
R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}
=
F_{\theta_1^\star}(X_i)-F_{\theta_1^\star}(X_i^{(-t)}).
\end{equation}
If \(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}>0\), removing the current step decreases the
predicted final value, which indicates that the step is supportive for the
final outcome.
In our framework, this counterfactual quantity is not used as a direct
training label; instead, it serves as an auxiliary consistency signal for the
local attribution.

\paragraph{Global Semantic Prior}

The local signal evaluates a step within one particular trajectory, but this
alone is not sufficient.
A step may look weak simply because it appears in a noisy or unfavorable
context, even though the same reasoning pattern is broadly useful across many
trajectories.
To capture this more intrinsic notion of step value, we complement local
credit with a global semantic prior estimated from the dataset.

Here we move from individual steps to recurring reasoning patterns.
From a Shapley perspective, one may view each semantic pattern as a player in
a cooperative game whose payoff is final trajectory success.
Its ideal value would be its average marginal contribution across different
co-occurrence contexts.
In practice, exact discrete Shapley computation over all pattern subsets is
intractable at dataset scale.
We therefore adopt a practical approximation that keeps the main effect of a
pattern relative to a global baseline.

Concretely, for each step \(s_{i,t}\), we retrieve the top-\(M\) semantically
nearest steps from other trajectories and use them to estimate how successful
this pattern tends to be.
Let \(\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)\) denote this neighbor set, and let
\(w_{j,\ell}\ge 0\) be the similarity-based weight assigned to neighbor
\((j,\ell)\).
We define the neighborhood success estimate as
\[
\hat r(s_{i,t})
=
\frac{
\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)} w_{j,\ell}r_j
}{
\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)} w_{j,\ell}
}.
\]
Comparing this quantity with the dataset-wide average reward
\(
\bar r=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}r_i
\),
we obtain the global prior
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_global}
\delta_{\mathrm{global}}(s_{i,t})
=
\hat r(s_{i,t})-\bar r.
\end{equation}
A positive value indicates that semantically similar steps tend to occur in
successful trajectories more often than average, while a negative value
suggests the opposite.
Although this quantity is only a practical approximation rather than an exact
Shapley value, it plays the role of a distribution-level marginal effect and
provides a stable complement to the local causal signal.
\subsection{Stage 3: Distilling Supervision into the Process Reward Model}
\label{stage3}

With the local and global signals in hand, we train a process reward model to
predict step quality directly from the reasoning trace.
For each trajectory, we insert a special boundary token
\(\langle\texttt{ST\_END}\rangle\) after the question and after every reasoning
step.
The model reads the entire sequence and outputs one reward prediction at each
boundary position for the immediately preceding segment.

We use a shared backbone with two task-specific heads, jointly parameterized by
\(\theta_3\).
At the boundary corresponding to step \(s_{i,t}\), the model predicts
\(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{local}}\) and
\(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{global}}\), which are trained against binarized local
and global targets,
\(
y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{local}}=\mathbf{1}(\delta_{\mathrm{local}}(s_{i,t})>0)
\)
and
\(
y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{global}}=\mathbf{1}(\delta_{\mathrm{global}}(s_{i,t})>0)
\).

For the local head, we further require consistency between the continuous
attribution and the discrete counterfactual check.
Specifically, we define a mask
\(
m_{i,t}
=
\mathbf{1}\!\left(
\delta_{\mathrm{local}}(s_{i,t})\cdot R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}>0
\right),
\)
and skip the local loss whenever \(m_{i,t}=0\).
In this way, local supervision is used only when both views agree on the
direction of the step's effect.

The training objective is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage3_objective}
\theta_3^\star
=
\arg\min_{\theta_3}
\left[
\lambda_{\mathrm{local}}
\sum_{i,t}
m_{i,t}\,
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(
\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{local}},y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{local}}
\right)
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{global}}
\sum_{i,t}
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(
\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{global}},y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{global}}
\right)
\right].
\end{equation}
The local head encourages the model to capture trajectory-specific causal
credit, while the global head injects a more stable semantic prior gathered
from the data distribution.

At inference time, we combine the two heads as
\(
\hat y_{i,t}
=
\lambda_{\mathrm{local}}\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{local}}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{global}}\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{global}}.
\)
The resulting score balances local causal evidence and global semantic
regularity, and can be used as the final step-level process reward.
