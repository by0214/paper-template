\section{Method}
We consider a dataset of multi-step reasoning trajectories,
$\mathcal{D}=\{(\tau_i,r_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$, where each trajectory is
$\tau_i=(question,s_{i,1},\ldots,s_{i,T_i})$ and the final outcome reward is
$r_i\in[0,1]$. Our goal is to transform sparse outcome supervision into
step-level supervision, and distill it into a large model such that, at each
step boundary, it can predict both the prior contribution of a
reasoning pattern and the contextual causal contribution of the
current step.

Our framework consists of three stages: learning a differentiable final-value
proxy, constructing step-level supervision signals, and training the process
reward model.

\subsection{Stage1: Learning Differentiable Final Reward Scorer}
\label{stage1}
To enable gradient-based Shapley approximation at the step level, we first
train a differentiable trajectory-level scorer
$F: X \xrightarrow{} \mathbb{R}$ to approximate the final reward $r$. We
represent each trajectory as $X_i=[x_{i,0},\ldots,x_{i,T_i}]$, where
$x_{i,t}\in\mathbb{R}^{d'}$ is the feature of step $t$. (Step-feature
selection is discussed in \ref{step_features_selection}.) The scorer outputs
$\hat{r} = F(X_i) \in \mathbb{R}$. We train $F$ with outcome supervision,
e.g., using binary cross-entropy:

\begin{equation}
\label{stage1_loss}
    \mathcal{L}_{stage1} = - \big[ r \log(\hat{r}) + (1-r) \log(1-\hat{r}) \big]
\end{equation}

The trained $F$ serves as the final-reward decomposer for subsequent
Shapley-style attribution.

\subsection{Stage2: Step-level Shapley Attribution}
\label{stage2}
We construct two complementary step-level signals: a pattern-level global
experience prior $\delta_{\text{global}}(s_{i,t})$, and an instance-level
local causal contribution $\delta_{\text{local}}^{\text{IG}}(s_{i,t})$. Both are derived
from ordered-Shapley marginal contributions.

\paragraph{Local Causal Shapley Attribution}
For a trajectory $\tau_i$, we treat each step feature $x_{i,t}$ as one player,
so $X_i$ can be viewed as the coalition of all players. We use integrated
gradients (IG) to compute step-wise marginal contributions. IG is equivalent to
Aumann--Shapley attribution and satisfies desirable axioms such as
completeness, symmetry, linearity, and sensitivity
\citep{sundararajan2017axiomatic}. Let $X_i^{\text{baseline}}$ be a baseline
trajectory such that $F(X_i^{\text{baseline}})\approx 0.5$ (e.g., an all-zero
vector; baseline choices are discussed in
\ref{ig_baseline_selection}). The step-level IG attribution can be written in
a single form as:
\begin{equation}
\label{ig_for_step}
    \delta_{\text{local}}^{\text{IG}}(s_{i,t})
    = \sum_{j \in s_{i,t}} \text{IG}_{i,t,j}
    = \sum_{j \in s_{i,t}} \left(x_{i,t,j} - x_{i,t,j}^{\text{baseline}}\right)
    \int_0^1 \frac{\partial F(\gamma_i(\alpha))}{\partial x_{i,t,j}} d\alpha
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \in [0,1]$ and $\gamma_i(\alpha)$ parameterizes the straight-line path from baseline to
input $\gamma_i(\alpha)=X_i^{\text{baseline}}+\alpha\left(X_i-X_i^{\text{baseline}}\right)$.
Here $\text{IG}_{i,t,j}$ denotes the Aumann--Shapley attribution on feature
dimension $j$ of step $t$, and the summation gives the step-level marginal
contribution by completeness.
This gives a continuous Aumann--Shapley estimate, but aggregating
dimension-level gradients can introduce semantic noise and blur key reasoning
steps. We therefore introduce a discrete counterfactual ablation term
$R_{\text{causal}}$ to filter noisy IG signals. Unlike pure temporal value
differences, this term measures the outcome change after masking the current
step while keeping the rest of the trajectory (including future steps):
\begin{equation}
\label{R_causal}
    R_{i,t}^{\text{causal}} = F(X_i^{(-t)}) - F(X_i)
\end{equation}
where $X_i^{(-t)}$ replaces $x_{i,t}$ with its baseline
$x_{i,t}^{\text{baseline}}$. This counterfactual score quantifies whether the
current step is causally supportive for the final outcome. In our framework,
$R_{i,t}^{\text{causal}}$ is not used as a direct supervision label; instead,
it is used in Stage~3 to filter unreliable local training signals.

\paragraph{Global Experience Shapley Prior}
We treat step-level ``semantic patterns'' (similarity clusters induced by
representations $x_{i,t}$) as players in a Shapley game, and estimate each
pattern's marginal effect on final success at the distribution level. Let the
global baseline success rate be
$
\bar r=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} r_i
$
which is the average value when all patterns co-occur. Computing exact Shapley
values over all pattern permutations is intractable on large datasets, so we
adopt a practical approximation. For any step $s_{i,t}$, we retrieve the top-$M$ semantically nearest steps across other trajectories:
$
\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)=\{(j,\ell)\mid j\neq i,\ \cos(x_{i,t},x_{j,\ell})\ \text{in top-}M\},
$
We then use the weighted success rate of this neighborhood as an empirical
estimate of value conditioned on this semantic pattern, and approximate its
distribution-level marginal effect by subtracting the baseline:
\begin{equation}
\label{delta_global}
\begin{split}
\delta_{\text{global}}(s_{i,t}) &= \hat r(s_{i,t})-\bar r = \frac{\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)} w_{j,\ell} r_j}{\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)} w_{j,\ell}} -\bar r
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $w_{j,\ell}$ is a non-negative weight mapped from similarity:
$
w_{j,\ell}=\kappa\!\left(\mathrm{sim}\big((i,t),(j,\ell)\big)\right),\quad \kappa(\cdot)\ge 0,
$
In practice, we use $\kappa(s)=\max(s,0)$, i.e., only positively similar
neighbors receive non-zero weights, which reduces noisy matches. Under this
definition, $\delta_{\text{global}}(s_{i,t})>0$ indicates that this semantic
pattern is historically associated with successful trajectories, while
$\delta_{\text{global}}(s_{i,t})<0$ indicates the opposite. Because
$\delta_{\text{global}}$ comes from cross-trajectory statistics rather than
within-trajectory counterfactual perturbations, it is complementary to
$\delta_{\text{local}}^{\text{IG}}$ and can be introduced as a cross-sample process prior
during distillation.

\subsection{Stage3: Training Reward Model}
\label{stage3}
Stage 3 distills the step-level supervision from Stage 2 into the base LLM so
that the model can output process rewards at step boundaries during inference.
For each trajectory, we insert a boundary token
$\langle\texttt{ST\_END}\rangle$ after the question end (discussed in
\ref{ablation_study}) and after every reasoning step. At each
$\langle\texttt{ST\_END}\rangle$ position, the model outputs a step-level
reward score for the preceding segment. A shared backbone with two
task-specific heads outputs
$(\hat y^{\text{local}}_{i,t},\hat y^{\text{global}}_{i,t})$, corresponding to
local and global supervision signals.

The two supervision sources are complementary:
$\delta_{\text{local}}^{\text{IG}}(s_{i,t})$ captures instance-level ordered causal
contribution, while $\delta_{\text{global}}(s_{i,t})$ captures
distribution-level semantic priors. Following prior findings
\citep{zhang-etal-2025-lessons, wang2024math, uesato2022solving}, we train
both heads with binarized BCE targets:
$y_{i,t}^{\text{local}}=\mathbf{1}(\delta_{\text{local}}^{\text{IG}}(s_{i,t})>0)$ and
$y_{i,t}^{\text{global}}=\mathbf{1}(\delta_{\text{global}}(s_{i,t})>0)$. For
the local head, we apply causal-consistency filtering:
$m_{i,t}=\mathbf{1}(\delta_{\text{local}}^{\text{IG}}(s_{i,t})\cdot R_{i,t}^{\text{causal}}>0)$.
When $m_{i,t}=0$, this boundary is skipped (no local loss is computed). The
Stage 3 objective is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage3_unified_loss}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{stage3}}=
\lambda_{\text{local}}\sum_{i,t}m_{i,t}\,\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(\hat y_{i,t}^{\text{local}},y_{i,t}^{\text{local}}\right)
+\lambda_{\text{global}}\sum_{i,t}\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(\hat y_{i,t}^{\text{global}},y_{i,t}^{\text{global}}\right)
\end{equation}
This dual-head strategy reduces signal confusion and cancellation between local
and global supervision, and improves representation capacity by preserving
their complementary learning targets. During inference, we use the same weights
to combine both heads:
$\hat y_{i,t}=\lambda_{\text{local}}\hat y_{i,t}^{\text{local}}+\lambda_{\text{global}}\hat y_{i,t}^{\text{global}}$,
which gives the final step-level reward score.
