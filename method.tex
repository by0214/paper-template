\section{Method}

We seek to learn step-level process supervision from reasoning trajectories that
only come with final outcome rewards.
The core difficulty is that the observed reward is defined on the full
trajectory rather than on individual steps.
Our solution is to construct two complementary supervision signals from the
trajectory data itself.
The first is the \emph{contextual contribution} of a step, \ie whether that
step helps or hurts the predicted outcome of the particular trajectory in which
it appears.
The second is the \emph{intrinsic value} of a reasoning pattern, \ie whether
that kind of step tends to correlate with successful reasoning across
trajectories.
These two views capture complementary aspects of process supervision:
one is trajectory-specific and causally grounded, while the other is
cross-trajectory and distributional.
SPRM distills both into a unified process reward model (PRM).

Starting from outcome-only reasoning trajectories, we first encode the question

We then learn a trajectory-level scorer that turns sparse outcome supervision

Figure~\ref{fig:sprm_overview} summarizes the full pipeline.

We first learn a differentiable trajectory scorer from outcome-only rewards.
This scorer turns sparse final outcomes into a trajectory value function on
which contextual step credit can be defined.
We then construct a local signal for trajectory-specific contribution and a
global signal for cross-trajectory pattern value, and finally distill both into
a dual-head PRM.

\subsection{Local Step Credit from Outcome-Only Trajectories}
\label{sec:method_local}

The local branch is derived from an ordered cooperative-game view of a
reasoning trajectory.
For each
\(
\tau_i=(q_i,s_{i,1},\ldots,s_{i,T_i})
\),
we treat the reasoning steps as players
\(
\mathcal{N}_i=\{1,\ldots,T_i\}
\)
that jointly produce the binary terminal payoff \(r_i\in\{0,1\}\).
The question is part of the trajectory context rather than a player.
To encode this context, we insert a special boundary token
\(\langle\texttt{ST\_END}\rangle\) after the question and after every reasoning
step, and use an encoder with sufficient semantic capacity to map the
trajectory into vector representations.
In our implementation, this encoder is the backbone LLM, and we use the
final-layer hidden state at each inserted boundary.
Let \(x_{i,0}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) denote the representation at the
\texttt{question\_end} position and \(x_{i,t}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) denote the
representation of step \(s_{i,t}\) for \(t\in\{1,\ldots,T_i\}\).
We write
\(
X_i=[x_{i,0},x_{i,1},\ldots,x_{i,T_i}]
\),
where \(x_{i,0}\) is retained during training as a contextual anchor because it
encodes the background information of the problem before any reasoning step is
taken.

To connect this representation to the Shapley framework, let
\(b\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) be a semantically uninformative baseline vector.
For any coalition \(S\subseteq\mathcal{N}_i\), define \(X_i^{S}\) as the
sequence that keeps \(x_{i,0}\) and all \(x_{i,t}\) with \(t\in S\), while
replacing the remaining step representations with \(b\).

\begin{definition}
For trajectory \(\tau_i\), the ordered cooperative game is
\(
\mathcal{G}_i=(\mathcal{N}_i,v_i)
\),
where each player corresponds to one reasoning step and
\(
v_i(S)
\)
denotes the trajectory value assigned to coalition \(S\).
Under temporal and causal constraints, the admissible coalitions are the prefix
sets
\(
P_{i,t}=\{1,\ldots,t\}
\)
for \(t\in\{0,\ldots,T_i\}\), with \(P_{i,0}=\emptyset\).
\end{definition}

This game makes precise what we mean by \emph{contextual contribution}: the
value of a step should be measured relative to the ordered prefix in which it
appears, rather than averaged over arbitrary permutations as in classical
Shapley values \citep{shapley1953value,pmlr-v119-sundararajan20b}.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:ordered_shapley_degenerate}
If only the causal order
\(
1\prec 2\prec \cdots \prec T_i
\)
is admissible, then the permutation-averaged discrete Shapley value for step
\(t\) degenerates to the ordered value difference
\begin{equation}
\phi_{i,t}^{\mathrm{ord}}
=
v_i(P_{i,t})-v_i(P_{i,t-1}).
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

Lemma~\ref{lem:ordered_shapley_degenerate} shows that once permutations are
collapsed by temporal causality, discrete Shapley credit reduces to a
prefix-wise value difference.
This is principled, but it is still a single hard perturbation of the value
function.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:reward_shaping}
Suppose the local process reward is defined only by the ordered value
difference
\(
r'_{i,t}=v_i(P_{i,t})-v_i(P_{i,t-1})
\).
Then
\(
\sum_{t=1}^{T_i} r'_{i,t}=v_i(P_{i,T_i})-v_i(P_{i,0})
\)
telescopes to a redistribution of the same trajectory-level signal.
Moreover, when \(v_i(P_{i,t})\) is instantiated by a potential function over
prefix states, \(r'_{i,t}\) is a potential-based reward shaping term and
therefore preserves the same optimal policy as the original sparse reward
\citep{ng1999policy}.
\end{proposition}

Proof sketches for
\Cref{lem:ordered_shapley_degenerate,prop:reward_shaping}
are deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_local_credit_proofs}.

Proposition~\ref{prop:reward_shaping} clarifies why ordered discrete
differences alone are insufficient as our final supervision target:
they mainly redistribute the sparse terminal reward and do not provide richer
process information beyond that signal.
Our goal is therefore not to use the discrete difference directly, but to
build a smoother and more informative local signal on top of a learned
trajectory value function.

In practice, the value function \(v_i\) is unknown and must be approximated
from outcome-only data.
We therefore learn a differentiable trajectory scorer
\(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}:\mathbb{R}^{(T_i+1)\times d}\rightarrow[0,1]\),
and interpret it as a learnable realization of the trajectory value function.
Its parameters are obtained from
\(
\mathcal{D}=\{(\tau_i,r_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}
\)
by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage1_objective}
\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star
=
\arg\min_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}
\mathbb{E}_{(\tau,r)\sim\mathcal{D}}
\left[
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}(X),r\right)
\right],
\end{equation}
where \(\mathrm{BCE}(\cdot,\cdot)\) denotes the binary cross-entropy loss with
binary target \(r\in\{0,1\}\).
After training, \(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}\) provides a differentiable
trajectory value estimate that serves as the practical realization of the game
value function.

Rather than using the discrete difference from
Lemma~\ref{lem:ordered_shapley_degenerate} as the supervision target, we use
the differentiability of \(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}\) to define a
continuous Aumann--Shapley credit signal.
Let \(b\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) be a semantically uninformative baseline vector, and
let \(B_i=[b,\ldots,b]\) be the corresponding baseline sequence with the same
shape as \(X_i\).
The interpolation path from baseline to observation is
\(
\gamma_i(\alpha)=B_i+\alpha(X_i-B_i)
\)
for \(\alpha\in[0,1]\).
Using Aumann--Shapley attribution implemented by integrated gradients
\citep{sundararajan2017axiomatic}, we define the local supervision signal for
step \(s_{i,t}\) as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_local}
\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}
=
\left(x_{i,t}-b\right)^\top
\int_0^1
\nabla_{x_{i,t}}
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}\!\left(\gamma_i(\alpha)\right)
\,d\alpha,
\qquad
t\in\{1,\ldots,T_i\}.
\end{equation}
This quantity measures whether the current step increases or decreases the
predicted value of the full trajectory when evaluated in its observed context,
without collapsing the signal to a single discrete prefix difference.

Integrated gradients provide a smooth estimate of ordered local credit, but the
signal still depends on a learned value function and can therefore be noisy.
We complement it with a discrete pairwise counterfactual check.
Let \(X_i^{(-t)}\) be the sequence obtained by replacing only \(x_{i,t}\) with
the baseline vector \(b\), and let \(X_i^{(-t,-u)}\) replace both \(x_{i,t}\)
and \(x_{i,u}\) with \(b\).
The direct counterfactual effect of step \(t\) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:direct_counterfactual}
R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{dir}}
=
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i)
-
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t)}).
\end{equation}
To model short-range dependencies between adjacent reasoning steps, we further
compute a pairwise interaction term
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_interaction}
I_{i,t,u}
=
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i)
-
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t)})
-
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-u)})
+
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t,-u)}),
\end{equation}
but only within an ordered local window
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_window}
\mathcal{W}(t)
=
\{u \mid 1 \le u < t,\; t-u \le w\},
\end{equation}
where \(w\) is a small window size.
We then construct deterministic interaction weights
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_weight}
\bar g_{i,t,u}
=
\frac{\mathbf{1}(u\in\mathcal{W}(t))\max(I_{i,t,u},0)}
{\sum_{v\in\mathcal{W}(t)} \max(I_{i,t,v},0)+\epsilon},
\end{equation}
and define the interaction-enhanced counterfactual score
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_counterfactual}
\tilde R_{i,t}
=
R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{dir}}
+
\beta\sum_{u\in\mathcal{W}(t)} \bar g_{i,t,u} I_{i,t,u},
\end{equation}
where \(\beta\) controls the strength of the pairwise correction.
In our framework, \(\tilde R_{i,t}\) is not used as the main local target.
Its role is to provide a discrete consistency check for the sign of
\(\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}\).

\subsection{Global Semantic Prior}
\label{sec:method_global}

The global branch targets a different quantity: the \emph{intrinsic value} of a
reasoning pattern across trajectories, namely whether that pattern tends to
correlate with successful reasoning beyond a single rollout.
A step can be locally helpful inside one trajectory yet instantiate a pattern
that is globally unreliable, and the reverse can also happen.
For this reason, the global branch does not follow the same ordered credit
logic as the local branch.
From a Shapley perspective, it is closer to retaining the main effect of a
semantic pattern relative to a dataset-level baseline.

We operationalize this idea with a semantic neighborhood prior.
For each step representation \(x_{i,t}\), we retrieve its top-\(M\) nearest
neighbors from other trajectories and denote the resulting set by
\(\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)\).
Let \(\omega_{i,t,j,\ell}\ge 0\) be the similarity-based weight assigned to
neighbor \((j,\ell)\in\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)\).
The neighborhood success estimate is
\[
\hat r_{i,t}
=
\frac{
\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)} \omega_{i,t,j,\ell} r_j
}{
\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{N}_M(i,t)} \omega_{i,t,j,\ell}
}.
\]
Comparing this quantity with the dataset-wide average reward
\(
\bar r=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} r_i
\),
we obtain the global supervision signal
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_global}
\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}
=
\hat r_{i,t}-\bar r.
\end{equation}
This quantity is a practical approximation to the marginal main effect of the
reasoning pattern represented by \(x_{i,t}\), and provides a stable complement
to the trajectory-specific local signal.

\subsection{Training the PRM and Inference}
\label{sec:method_prm}

The local and global signals above are used to supervise the final PRM at the
positions corresponding to reasoning steps.
The PRM reads the same boundary-augmented sequence and uses a shared backbone
with two task-specific heads, jointly parameterized by
\(\theta_{\mathrm{PRM}}\).
For step \(s_{i,t}\), the two heads predict
\(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}\in(0,1)\) and
\(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}\in(0,1)\).
We convert the constructed signals into binary targets
\[
y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}=\mathbf{1}\!\left(\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}>0\right),
\qquad
y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}=\mathbf{1}\!\left(\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}>0\right).
\]

For the local head, we only trust supervision when the continuous attribution
and the discrete counterfactual check agree on the sign of the step effect.
We therefore define the consistency mask
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:local_mask}
m_{i,t}
=
\mathbf{1}\!\left(
\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}} \cdot \tilde R_{i,t} > 0
\right).
\end{equation}
The PRM objective is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage3_objective}
\theta_{\mathrm{PRM}}^\star
=
\arg\min_{\theta_{\mathrm{PRM}}}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{E}_{(\tau_i,r_i)\sim\mathcal{D}}
\Big[
&\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}
m_{i,t}\,
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}, y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}\right) \\
&+
\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}, y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}\right)
\Big],
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where the local branch captures trajectory-specific contextual credit and the
global branch injects a cross-trajectory semantic prior.
Because the two heads are trained jointly with a shared backbone, we do not
introduce an explicit mixing weight in the training loss.

At inference time, we combine the two heads as
\[
\hat y_{i,t}
=
\lambda \hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}
+
(1-\lambda)\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}},
\]
where \(\lambda\in[0,1]\) controls the trade-off between the local and global
heads at inference time.
The resulting score balances local causal evidence with global semantic
regularity and serves as the final step-level reward.
