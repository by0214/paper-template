\section{Method}

We learn step-level process supervision from reasoning trajectories with only
final outcome rewards.
The challenge is that reward is observed on the full trajectory rather than on
individual steps.
SPRM therefore constructs two complementary signals from the trajectory data
itself: the \emph{contextual contribution} of a step, \ie whether it helps or
hurts the predicted outcome of its trajectory, and the \emph{intrinsic value}
of a reasoning pattern, \ie whether it tends to correlate with success across
trajectories.
The former is trajectory-specific and causal; the latter is cross-trajectory
and distributional.
SPRM distills both into a unified process reward model (PRM).

The local channel defines step value under temporal and causal constraints,

\subsection{Local Step Credit from Outcome-Only Trajectories}
\label{sec:method_local}

To construct trajectory-specific local credit from outcome-only rewards, we
start from a cooperative game over reasoning steps.
Consider a dataset
\(
\mathcal{D}=\{(\tau_i,r_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}
\),
where
\(
\tau_i=(q_i,s_{i,1},\ldots,s_{i,T_i})
\)
is a reasoning trajectory and \(r_i\in\{0,1\}\) is its final reward.
For each trajectory, the reasoning steps are treated as players
\(
\mathcal{N}_i=\{s_{i,1},\ldots,s_{i,T_i}\}
\),
and a coalition \(S\subseteq\mathcal{N}_i\) is assigned value \(v(S)\).
Under the classical discrete Shapley formulation, the credit of step \(t\) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:discrete_shapley}
\phi_{i,t}^{\mathrm{sha}}
=
\frac{1}{T_i!}
\sum_{\pi\in\Pi(\mathcal{N}_i)}
\Big[
v\!\big(\mathrm{Pre}_{\pi}(s_{i,t})\cup\{s_{i,t}\}\big)
-
v\!\big(\mathrm{Pre}_{\pi}(s_{i,t})\big)
\Big],
\end{equation}
where \(\Pi(\mathcal{N}_i)\) is the set of all permutations of
\(\mathcal{N}_i\), and \(\mathrm{Pre}_{\pi}(s_{i,t})\) denotes the predecessors of
step \(t\) under permutation \(\pi\).
This is the expected marginal contribution of a step over all admissible
insertion orders \citep{shapley1953value,pmlr-v119-sundararajan20b}.

However, reasoning trajectories are temporally ordered and causally
constrained, so arbitrary permutations are not admissible.
Let \(P_{i,t}\) be a permutation of \(\mathcal{N}_i\) that orders the steps in the observed causal order,
\(
P_{i,t}=\{s_{i,1},\ldots,s_{i,t}\}
\)
for \(t\in\{1,\ldots,T_i\}\), with \(P_{i, 0} = \emptyset\).

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:ordered_shapley_degenerate}
If only the observed causal order
\(
1\prec 2\prec \cdots \prec T_i
\)
is admissible, then the discrete Shapley value in
\Cref{eq:discrete_shapley} degenerates to
\begin{equation}
\phi_{i,t}^{\mathrm{ord}}
=
v(P_{i,t})-v(P_{i,t-1}).
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\textit{Proof sketch.}
Under a strict order, \(\mathrm{Pre}_{\pi}(t)\) degenerates to
\(P_{i,t-1}\).
Substituting this into \Cref{eq:discrete_shapley} directly yields the value
difference above.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:reward_shaping_invariance}
Let \(v^\star(P_{i,t})\) denote the optimal trajectory value on prefix state
\(P_{i,t}\), and define the dense step reward
\(
\tilde r_{i,t}^\star
=
v^\star(P_{i,t})-v^\star(P_{i,t-1}).
\)
Then a PRM trained on this value-difference reward induces the same optimal
policy as the original sparse terminal reward.
\end{lemma}

Formal proof of \Cref{lem:reward_shaping_invariance} is deferred to
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_local_credit_proofs}.
The key observation is that \(\tilde r_{i,t}^\star\) is a potential-based
reward shaping term over prefix states and therefore preserves the optimal
policy under the reward-transformation theorem of
\citep{ng1999policy}.

These lemmas indicate that directly applying Shapley values to temporally ordered trajectories 
fails to address the limitations of sparse rewards, 
while introducing a strong dependence on the choice of value model.
We therefore keep the same fairness intuition but replace discrete differences
with a lightweight trajectory scorer, IG-based marginal attribution, and
pairwise causal filtering to derive stronger local supervision from
outcome-only data.

For each trajectory, we encode the question and all reasoning steps into
\(
X_i=[x_{i,0},x_{i,1},\ldots,x_{i,T_i}]
\in\mathbb{R}^{(T_i+1)\times d},
\)
where \(x_{i,0}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) is the representation of question \(q_i\),
which we denote as \textit{question\_end}, and
\(x_{i,t}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) is the representation of step \(s_{i,t}\).
These representations are produced by an encoder, such as an embedding model
or LLM backbone.

On top of these representations, we learn a lightweight trajectory scorer
\(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}:\mathbb{R}^{(T_i+1)\times d}\rightarrow[0,1]\)
from scratch on \(\mathcal{D}\) and use it as a learnable approximation to
\(v\).
Its role is to provide a differentiable trajectory value model on which step
marginal contribution can be defined, rather than to act as an external judge.
The scorer is trained directly from step representations and final reward:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stage1_objective}
\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star
=
\arg\min_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}
\mathbb{E}_{(\tau,r)\sim\mathcal{D}}
\left[
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}}(\mathrm{encoder}(\tau)),r\right)
\right],
\end{equation}
where \(\mathrm{BCE}(\cdot,\cdot)\) denotes the binary cross-entropy loss with
binary target \(r\in\{0,1\}\).

\(F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}\) yields a smooth trajectory value surface
from which we compute each step's marginal contribution.
We interpret how a step's features raise or lower predicted reward as its
contribution to the current trajectory.
Gradient integration\citep{sundararajan2017axiomatic} quantifies this by
integrating gradients along a path from a semantically uninformative baseline
to the observed representation.
Let \(b\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) be a semantically uninformative baseline vector, and
let \(B_i=[b,\ldots,b]\) be the corresponding baseline sequence with the same
shape as \(X_i\).
The interpolation path from baseline to observation is
\(
\gamma_i(\alpha)=B_i+\alpha(X_i-B_i)
\)
for \(\alpha\in[0,1]\).
Using this path, we define the marginal contribution of step \(s_{i,t}\) as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_local}
\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}
=
\left(x_{i,t}-b\right)^\top
\int_0^1
\nabla_{x_{i,t}}
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}\!\left(\gamma_i(\alpha)\right)
\,d\alpha,
\qquad
t\in\{1,\ldots,T_i\}.
\end{equation}
This IG-based measure is equivalent to the Aumann--Shapley
value\citep{sundararajan2017axiomatic}.
Unlike ordered prefix differences, it remains sensitive to the full inference
chain and yields smoother marginal estimates.
At the same time, following recent consistency-based approaches
\citep{zheng2025processbench, wang2025athena}, we introduce a pairwise
counterfactual causal-consistency mechanism and later use it in
\Cref{sec:method_prm} to filter unreliable local supervision.

Let \(X_i^{(-t)}\) be the sequence obtained by replacing only \(x_{i,t}\) with
the baseline vector \(b\), and let \(X_i^{(-t,-u)}\) replace both \(x_{i,t}\)
and \(x_{i,u}\) with \(b\).
\begin{align}
\label{eq:direct_counterfactual}
R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{dir}}
&=
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i)
-
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t)}), \\
\label{eq:pairwise_interaction}
R_{i,(t,u)}^{\mathrm{int}}
&=
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i)
-
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t)})
-
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-u)})
+
F_{\theta_{\mathrm{scorer}}^\star}(X_i^{(-t,-u)}).
\end{align}
These counterfactual perturbations capture causality by masking steps and
measuring the resulting value change: \(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{dir}}\) measures the
effect of invalidating one step, while \(R_{i,(t,u)}^{\mathrm{int}}\) measures
their joint effect.
To obtain a reliable consistency signal, we aggregate pairwise interactions
within an ordered local window \(\mathcal{W}(t)=\{u \mid 1 \le u < t,\; t-u \le w\}\)
and define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pairwise_counterfactual}
R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}
=
R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{dir}}
+
\beta
\frac{
\sum_{u\in\mathcal{W}(t)} \max\!\big(0, R_{i,(t,u)}^{\mathrm{int}}\big)\,
R_{i,(t,u)}^{\mathrm{int}}
}{
\sum_{u\in\mathcal{W}(t)} \max\!\big(0, R_{i,(t,u)}^{\mathrm{int}}\big)
+ \epsilon
},
\end{equation}
where \(\beta\) controls the strength of the pairwise correction.
This emphasizes positively synergistic pairs: erroneous steps tend to propagate
and are therefore discarded, while key logical transitions are sparse and thus
highlighted by \(\max(0,\cdot)\).
Although pairwise checks are local, via logical transitivity they also provide a
tractable proxy for longer-range dependencies.
The local branch still uses \(\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}\) as its primary
contextual supervision, while \(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\) only decides
whether that supervision is reliable enough to keep.

\subsection{Global Semantic Prior}
\label{sec:method_global}

The global branch targets a different quantity: the \emph{intrinsic value} of a
reasoning pattern across trajectories, \ie whether it correlates with success
beyond a single rollout.
A step that is correct in itself can still appear inside an unsuccessful
trajectory, for example when it follows a mistaken earlier conclusion and is
therefore embedded in an incorrect reasoning chain.
The goal is to estimate this intrinsic step value across contexts, making the
reward signal less sensitive to trajectory-local variation and hence more
stable.
For this reason, the global branch does not follow the same ordered credit
logic as the local branch.

We operationalize this idea with a semantic neighborhood prior.
For each step representation \(x_{i,t}\), we retrieve its top-\(M\) nearest
neighbors from other trajectories and denote the resulting set by
\(\mathcal{H}_M(i,t)\).
Let \(\omega_{i,t,j,\ell}\ge 0\) be the similarity-based weight assigned to
neighbor \((j,\ell)\in\mathcal{H}_M(i,t)\).
The neighborhood success estimate is
\[
\hat r_{i,t}
=
\frac{
\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{H}_M(i,t)} \omega_{i,t,j,\ell} r_j
}{
\sum_{(j,\ell)\in\mathcal{H}_M(i,t)} \omega_{i,t,j,\ell}
}.
\]
Comparing this quantity with the dataset-wide average reward
\(
\bar r=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} r_i
\),
we obtain the global supervision signal
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_global}
\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}
=
\hat r_{i,t}-\bar r.
\end{equation}
This quantity measures whether the semantic neighborhood of \(x_{i,t}\) is more
or less associated with success than the dataset average, providing a stable
complement to the trajectory-specific local signal.

\subsection{Training the PRM and Inference}
\label{sec:method_prm}

The local and global signals above supervise the final PRM at reasoning-step
positions.
The PRM reads the same boundary-augmented sequence and uses a shared backbone
with two task-specific heads, jointly parameterized by
\(\theta_{\mathrm{PRM}}\).
For step \(s_{i,t}\), the two heads predict
\(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}\in(0,1)\) and
\(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}\in(0,1)\).
We convert the constructed signals into binary targets
\[
y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}=\mathbf{1}\!\left(\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}>0\right),
\qquad
y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}=\mathbf{1}\!\left(\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}>0\right).
\]

For the local head, we trust supervision only when the IG-based local credit
and the pairwise counterfactual consistency check agree on the sign of the step
effect.
We therefore define the consistency mask
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:local_mask}
m_{i,t}
=
\mathbf{1}\!\left(
\delta_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}} \cdot R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}} > 0
\right).
\end{equation}
The PRM objective is
\begin{align}
\label{eq:stage3_objective}
\theta_{\mathrm{PRM}}^\star
&=
\arg\min_{\theta_{\mathrm{PRM}}}
\mathbb{E}_{(\tau_i,r_i)\sim\mathcal{D}}
\Big[
\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}
m_{i,t}\,
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}, y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}\right)
\notag\\
&\quad+
\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}
\mathrm{BCE}\!\left(\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}, y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}}\right)
\Big],
\end{align}
where the local branch captures trajectory-specific contextual credit and the
global branch injects a cross-trajectory semantic prior.
Because the two heads are trained jointly with a shared backbone, we introduce
no explicit mixing weight in the training loss.

At inference time, we combine the two heads as
\[
\hat y_{i,t}
=
\lambda \hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{loc}}
+
(1-\lambda)\hat y_{i,t}^{\mathrm{glo}},
\]
where \(\lambda\in[0,1]\) controls the local-global trade-off at inference
time.
The resulting score balances local causal evidence with global semantic
regularity and serves as the final step-level reward.
