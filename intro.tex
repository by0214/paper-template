\section{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong reasoning ability on
tasks such as mathematical proof generation, complex question answering, and
code synthesis.
Yet their training and alignment are still dominated by outcome-level
supervision: rewards are typically assigned only to the final answer, while
intermediate steps in long reasoning chains receive little direct feedback.
This sparse-reward setting creates a severe temporal credit-assignment
problem.
A model can observe whether a trajectory eventually succeeds, but it cannot
directly tell which steps were responsible for that success
\citep{uesato2022solving, lightman2023lets}.
As a result, process reward models (PRMs), which provide fine-grained
step-level feedback, have become an important component in test-time scaling,
reranking, and reinforcement learning for LLM reasoning.

The main bottleneck in current PRM development is therefore not the model
architecture itself, but the acquisition of high-quality step-level
supervision.
Human annotation is reliable but expensive and difficult to scale.
Monte Carlo-based labeling is more automatic, but it requires large rollout
budgets and often remains noisy.
LLM-as-a-judge further reduces annotation cost, yet its labels can inherit
biases from pretraining data and be sensitive to prompt wording or scoring
criteria, which makes the resulting supervision unstable.
More broadly, these pipelines still depend on external annotators or
evaluators to tell the model which intermediate steps are good.
This dependence limits robustness, scalability, and reuse.
Meanwhile, outcome-level reinforcement learning methods such as GRPO can
optimize policies from final rewards, but they do not directly produce a
reusable source of step-level supervision.
For long-horizon reasoning, the central challenge remains how to transform a
single final outcome into reliable credit for individual reasoning steps.

This challenge leads us to ask whether step-level credit can be derived
directly from trajectory data itself, without relying on external judges.
A multi-step reasoning trajectory that jointly yields a final reward naturally
resembles a cooperative game with a shared payoff.
This analogy points to Shapley values, which allocate total payoff by marginal
contribution under a principled fairness criterion.
From this perspective, Shapley-style attribution offers an appealing bridge
from sparse outcome rewards to dense process supervision.
However, reasoning trajectories differ fundamentally from the classical
Shapley setting.
Reasoning steps are temporally ordered, causally dependent, and not freely
exchangeable.
Under strict causal constraints,
naively applying classical Shapley can
degenerate into prefix-wise value differences along the original sequence,
which mainly redistributes the same sparse outcome signal and remains sensitive
to value-estimation noise.
As a result, it fails to capture non-local interactions across steps in a
usefully dense form.
Therefore, the key design problem is not merely to adopt Shapley values, but
to adapt their fairness intuition to ordered, causally constrained reasoning
processes.

Motivated by this goal, we propose \textbf{SPRM}
(\textbf{S}hapley-value based \textbf{P}rocess \textbf{R}eward
\textbf{M}odel), a framework that automatically constructs trainable
step-level supervision from outcome-only signals.
Our central idea is to treat a reasoning trajectory as an ordered cooperative
process and to decompose its final reward into structured step-level credit
without external annotation.
Concretely, we first learn a trajectory-level scorer that predicts the final
trajectory reward from the whole reasoning chain.
This scorer serves as a differentiable trajectory value function, which turns
sparse outcome supervision into a form on which step-level credit can be
defined.
We then estimate instance-level local credit by combining a path-integrated
attribution mechanism inspired by Aumann--Shapley with a pairwise
counterfactual consistency check, which together measure how each step changes
the predicted value of the trajectory.
Because the trajectory scorer is differentiable, we can define this local
credit through path-integrated attribution rather than relying only on the
degenerate ordered value difference, yielding a smoother estimate of each step's
effect on the predicted trajectory value.
In parallel, we estimate distribution-level global priors from semantic
neighbor statistics across trajectories, capturing whether a step pattern tends
to correlate with successful reasoning beyond one specific rollout.
These local and global signals are jointly distilled into a shared PRM
backbone through a dual-head objective, balancing causal specificity with
cross-trajectory stability.

This design directly addresses the limitations of existing PRM supervision
pipelines.
Unlike annotation-based\cite{uesato2022solving,lightman2023lets} or judge-based approaches\cite{versaprm, duan2025efficient,yang2025beyond,wang2025athena,cao2025dreamprm}, SPRM derives supervision
directly from sparse outcome signals and therefore removes the need for human
step labels or external evaluators.
Unlike purely local counterfactual scoring, it combines continuous local credit
with a discrete counterfactual check and global semantic priors, making the
resulting labels more
stable and less sensitive to noise in any single trajectory.
Unlike outcome-level RL alone, SPRM produces reusable step-level reward
signals that can support PRM training, reranking, and test-time reasoning.
Overall, SPRM aims to provide a more coherent trade-off among automation,
causal grounding, and scalability for process supervision in LLM reasoning.

Our main contributions are summarized as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose an annotation-free automatic labeling framework that
    converts outcome-only rewards in LLM reasoning into trainable step-level
    supervision, without relying on human annotation or external judges.
    \item We revisit step credit assignment from a cooperative-game
    perspective, and introduce a Shapley-inspired attribution principle
    tailored to temporally ordered and causally constrained reasoning
    trajectories.
    \item We present an end-to-end SPRM training framework that unifies local
    trajectory-level attribution and global cross-trajectory priors, and
    validate its effectiveness through comprehensive analyses and experiments on
    ProcessBench, PRMBench and Best-of-\(N\) benchmarks.
\end{itemize}
