\section{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong reasoning ability on tasks such as mathematical proof generation, complex question answering, and code synthesis. Yet their training and alignment are still dominated by outcome-level supervision: rewards are typically assigned only at the final answer, while intermediate steps in long reasoning chains receive little direct feedback. This sparse-reward setting creates a severe temporal credit-assignment problem---the model can tell whether the final result is correct, but not which reasoning steps actually caused that success \citep{uesato2022solving, lightman2023lets}. As a result, process reward models (PRMs), which provide fine-grained step-level feedback, have become a key component in test-time scaling, reranking, and reinforcement learning.

However, the primary bottleneck in current PRM development is the automated acquisition of high-quality step-level supervision. Human annotation is reliable but expensive and hard to scale. Monte Carlo-based labeling is more automatic, but it requires large rollout budgets and still suffers from noise. LLM-as-a-judge can further reduce annotation cost, yet its labels may inherit biases from pretraining data and are sensitive to prompt wording and scoring criteria, leading to unstable supervision. As a result, existing PRM pipelines still rely heavily on external annotators or evaluators, which limits robust large-scale reuse.

This strong external dependence motivates a different question: can we derive step-level credit from the data itself? A multi-step trajectory that jointly yields a final reward naturally resembles a cooperative game with a shared payoff. This analogy leads to Shapley values, which allocate total payoff by marginal contribution under fairness axioms and thus provide a principled bridge from sparse outcome rewards to step-level supervision. However, classical Shapley assumes unordered and exchangeable players, while reasoning steps are temporally ordered and causally dependent. Under strict temporal constraints, naively applying classical Shapley degenerates into prefix value differences along the original sequence, which remains sensitive to value-estimation noise and misses non-local interactions.

This motivates a concrete design goal: preserve the fairness intuition of Shapley while adapting attribution to ordered, causally constrained trajectories. Motivated by this goal, we propose \textbf{SPRM} (\textbf{S}hapley-value based \textbf{P}rocess \textbf{R}eward \textbf{M}odel), a framework that automatically constructs trainable step-level supervision from outcome-only signals. Our central idea is to treat a reasoning trajectory as an ordered, causally constrained cooperative process, and to decompose the final reward in a structured Shapley-inspired manner rather than relying on external step annotations. Concretely, we first learn a differentiable proxy of final value, then combine Aumann--Shapley-style gradient attribution with discrete counterfactual ablation to obtain instance-level local causal contributions. In parallel, we extract distribution-level global priors from semantic-neighbor statistics across trajectories. These two signals are jointly distilled into a shared PRM backbone through a dual-head objective, balancing local causality and global stability.

Building on the above formulation, SPRM is designed with three core properties. \textbf{First}, it requires no human step annotations or external judges, and derives supervision directly from sparse outcome signals. \textbf{Second}, it jointly models two complementary aspects of step quality: causal contribution to final success (local counterfactual effect) and intrinsic step value estimated from cross-trajectory semantics (global prior). \textbf{Third}, it grounds credit assignment in a Shapley-theoretic fair allocation principle adapted to ordered, causally constrained reasoning trajectories. Together, these properties provide a more coherent balance of automation, reliability, and scalability.

Our main contributions are summarized as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose a high-quality automatic labeling method for the sparse-reward problem in LLM reasoning, converting outcome-only supervision into trainable step-level reward signals without manual annotation.
    \item We revisit step credit assignment from a cooperative-game perspective, and introduce a Shapley-based fairness principle for reward decomposition under temporal and causal constraints.
    \item We present an end-to-end SPRM training framework that unifies local causal attribution and global intrinsic-value estimation, and provide comprehensive analyses and experiments on ProcessBench and PRMBench.
\end{itemize}
