\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}
In the main text, we retain only analyses that directly support our core
claims, namely why SPRM benefits from additive supervision components, why
filtering is necessary for local credit assignment, and why local and global
signals should be designed differently. Implementation-focused analyses,
including Stage 1 model selection, input feature selection, and IG baseline
selection, are deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix}.

\label{ablation_study}
Under fixed backbone, training-data scale, and optimization hyperparameters, we
perform additive ablations over the key SPRM modules.

Figure~\ref{fig:ablation_all_metrics} reports four variants under a unified
protocol:
(1) IG-only local supervision without the question-boundary state \(x_{i,0}\);
(2) IG with the question-boundary state;
(3) IG with the question-boundary state and
\(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\) filtering; and
(4) the full SPRM that further adds \(\delta_{\mathrm{global}}\).

Three observations are consistent across metrics.
First, introducing the question-boundary state improves boundary calibration,
showing that local attribution should not start from the first reasoning step
alone.
The transition from the problem statement to the reasoning chain already
contains information about how subsequent steps should be interpreted, so
including \(x_{i,0}\) gives the scorer a more stable anchor for the first
credit-assignment decision.
Second, adding \(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\) further stabilizes training and
improves the balance between \textit{Err} and \textit{Cor}, which indicates
that raw IG-based local signals contain non-trivial noise and benefit from an
agreement check against discrete counterfactual perturbation.
Third, incorporating \(\delta_{\mathrm{global}}\) brings the strongest gains on
the harder domains, especially OLYMPIADBENCH and OMNIMATH.
Although \textit{Cor} may slightly decrease in some settings, the overall F1
improves, yielding the best trade-off in the full model.

Overall, the ablation confirms that SPRM's gain does not come from a single
module.
The question-boundary state improves step alignment, causal filtering reduces
label noise in local supervision, and the global prior adds robustness against
context-dependent fluctuations in step value.

\subsection{Filtering Strategy Comparison}
We next ask two questions:
is filtering itself necessary for local supervision, and if so, why do we use
\(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\) rather than an alternative LLM-consistency
filter?
To answer them, we compare the two filtering strategies under the same
evaluation protocol.

\begin{figure*}[t]\todo{use table}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/filter_all_metrics.pdf}
\caption{Impact of the filtering strategy on all evaluation metrics. The plot
reports performance differences between variants with and without filtering
strategies.}
\label{fig:filter_all_metrics}
\end{figure*}

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:filter_all_metrics}, both filters improve over
using raw local attribution alone, which shows that a filtering stage is
necessary rather than optional.
This is consistent with the role of \(\delta_{\mathrm{local}}\): it is derived
from a learned value proxy through a path-integrated attribution procedure, so
its sign can fluctuate when the scorer is imperfect or when long trajectories
amplify local noise.
Filtering therefore acts as a reliability test before the local signal is used
as supervision.

The two filters, however, emphasize different notions of reliability.
The LLM-consistency filter is more aligned with semantic agreement and can
sharpen sensitivity-oriented indicators such as first-error localization.
By contrast, \(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\) directly asks whether removing the
current step changes the predicted trajectory value, so it is more tightly
aligned with the contextual contribution that the local head is intended to
capture.
This leads to more stable gains on correct-path acceptance and stronger
behavior on the harder long-horizon benchmarks.

This comparison clarifies why the final method uses
\(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\).
The LLM-consistency filter is a useful empirical reference because it confirms
that denoising local credit matters, but the counterfactual filter is more
faithful to our target quantity: whether the current step helps or hurts the
outcome of the specific trajectory in which it appears.

\subsection{Why Local and Global Use Different Attribution Designs?}\todo{It feels abrupt here; I suggest removing it and condensing it into a "theory" section within the "Methods."}
The design of SPRM starts from a simple question:
what makes a reasoning step good or bad?
We decompose the answer into two complementary aspects.
One is the step's \emph{contextual contribution}, namely whether it promotes or
suppresses success in the specific trajectory where it appears.
The other is the step's \emph{intrinsic value}, namely whether the underlying
reasoning pattern tends to be useful across trajectories.
These two notions should not be conflated.
A good step can appear inside a failed trajectory, while a flawed step can be
locally masked inside a successful one.
Using only one view therefore makes supervision brittle.

The local signal should preserve temporal order and causal dependence.
For a trajectory \(\tau=(s_1,\dots,s_T)\), steps are not freely exchangeable:
later steps depend on earlier ones, and only the observed order is causally
valid.
Under this constraint, the classical discrete Shapley view over permutations
collapses to an ordered marginal form:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ordered_shapley_analysis}
\phi_t^{\mathrm{ord}}
:=
v_t-v_{t-1},
\qquad
v_t:=v(\{1,2,\dots,t\}),
\;
v_0:=v(\emptyset).
\end{equation}

This observation explains why the local signal should follow an ordered
Shapley intuition instead of an unordered one.
At the same time, the discrete difference in
\eqref{eq:ordered_shapley_analysis} is too brittle to serve as the final local
supervision signal by itself:
it depends strongly on scorer calibration and captures only one hard marginal
change.
We therefore use Aumann--Shapley attribution through integrated gradients as a
continuous relaxation of ordered credit assignment, and pair it with
\(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\) as a discrete counterfactual check.
The former provides a smooth local contribution estimate, while the latter
keeps the sign aligned with explicit step removal.

The global signal answers a different question, so it need not obey the same
ordering constraint.
Here the object of interest is no longer a position in one trajectory, but a
semantic reasoning pattern that may recur across many trajectories.
Let \(\mathcal{P}\) denote a set of such patterns and let \(z\in\mathcal{P}\)
be one candidate pattern.
Because these patterns are compared through cross-trajectory co-occurrence
rather than within-trajectory temporal order, the classical discrete Shapley
view becomes appropriate again:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:global_shapley_analysis}
\phi(z)
=
\sum_{S\subseteq\mathcal{P}\setminus\{z\}}
\frac{|S|!\left(|\mathcal{P}|-|S|-1\right)!}{|\mathcal{P}|!}
\bigl[v(S\cup\{z\})-v(S)\bigr].
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:global_shapley_analysis} averages the marginal contribution
of pattern \(z\) over all co-occurrence contexts \(S\).
Its exact computation is infeasible at dataset scale, so we retain only the
main effect relative to the global baseline:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:global_main_effect_analysis}
\phi^{\mathrm{main}}(z)
:=
v(\{z\})-v(\emptyset).
\end{equation}
Our global prior
\(\delta_{\mathrm{global}}(s_{i,t})=\hat r(s_{i,t})-\bar r\) is precisely a
practical approximation to this main effect:
the KNN neighborhood estimates how successful the semantic pattern associated
with \(s_{i,t}\) tends to be, and \(\bar r\) plays the role of the null
baseline.

In this sense, local and global are not two redundant views of the same
quantity.
The local signal estimates how the current step affects the present trajectory
under temporal and causal constraints, whereas the global signal estimates the
intrinsic value of the underlying reasoning pattern without such constraints.
Their combination is therefore necessary: local credit provides causal
specificity, and global value provides robustness against context-induced
fluctuations.
