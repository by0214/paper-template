\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}
In the main text, we retain only analyses that directly support our core
claims: why prefix value differences are insufficient as learned PRM
supervision, how SPRM constructs reliable local credit from outcome-only
trajectories, and how the global reward-reshaping prior stabilizes that signal.
Implementation-focused analyses, including Stage 1 model selection, input
feature selection, IG baseline selection, and extended neighborhood
diagnostics, are deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix}.

\subsection{Ablation I: Local Credit and Consistency}
\label{ablation_study}
Under fixed backbone, training-data scale, and optimization hyperparameters, we
perform targeted ablations over the key SPRM mechanisms.

Table~\ref{tab:ablation_processbench} organizes the ablation into four
blocks: local credit construction, consistency gating, the global-prior
branch, and head mixing.

The local-credit rows first compare three ways of forming local credit.
Using the degenerate ordered value difference alone as supervision substantially
underperforms the IG-based local signal.
This does not contradict the policy-preserving interpretation in
\Cref{sec:formulation}; rather, it shows that a principled prefix
redistribution is not by itself a reliable learned PRM label under imperfect
value estimates.
Removing the \texttt{question\_end} representation also causes a clear drop,
showing that local attribution needs the problem-to-reasoning transition as an
explicit boundary anchor.
These results motivate the final IG-based local credit design.

The causal-consistency-check rows then ask how to enhance the IG-based local signal.
For the LLM-consistency mechanism, labels are generated by DeepSeek-V3.2 (2025.12.01).
Both consistency mechanisms substantially improve over using raw IG signs
directly, confirming that local credit needs an explicit reliability check
before serving as supervision.
The two mechanisms tie on average F1, but with a different trade-off: LLM
consistency rejects errors more aggressively, whereas
\(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\)-based consistency preserves much stronger
\textit{Cor}.
This matches the role of \(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\) in
\Cref{eq:pairwise_counterfactual}, which retains direct causal effect while
rewarding coherent multi-step support.
We therefore use \(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\) in the final method.

\subsection{Ablation II: Global Prior and Head Complementarity}
The last two blocks of Table~\ref{tab:ablation_processbench} ask a different
question: what does the global reward-reshaping prior add beyond local credit,
and does that gain come from semantic neighborhood structure rather than
generic smoothing?

The global-prior rows show that reward reshaping is complementary rather than
substitutive.
``Global only'' is much weaker than ``Local'', which indicates that
cross-trajectory regularities alone cannot replace trajectory-specific credit.
At the same time, ``Local + global'' achieves the best average F1 by trading a
modest drop in \textit{Cor} for a much larger gain in \textit{Err}, exactly
the trade-off expected when global semantic priors suppress locally plausible
but globally unreliable patterns.
The random-neighbor control further shows that this gain does not come from
adding an arbitrary smoothing branch: replacing semantic retrieval with random
neighbors slightly hurts both \textit{Cor} and overall F1 relative to
``Local'', whereas the semantic global branch produces a much larger F1 gain.

The head-mixing rows then show that head complementarity depends on balance.
A small amount of global mixing can improve correct-path acceptance, as seen
from the strong \textit{Cor} at
\(\lambda_{\mathrm{loc}}=0.75,\lambda_{\mathrm{glo}}=0.25\), while the balanced
setting
\(\lambda_{\mathrm{loc}}=0.50,\lambda_{\mathrm{glo}}=0.50\) recovers the best
overall F1 and strongest \textit{Err}.
By contrast, placing too much weight on the global head sharply weakens both
\textit{Err} and overall F1.
This pattern matches the intended division of labor: the local branch provides
trajectory-specific credit, while the global branch reshapes the reward with a
stabilizing prior rather than replacing credit assignment.

This interpretation is also supported by the auxiliary neighborhood diagnostic:
the trajectory-mean \(\delta^{\mathrm{glo}}\) achieves an AUC of 0.856 against
final reward, with Pearson correlations of 0.613 to trajectory reward and
0.510 to step labels, while random-neighbor and reward-shuffle baselines are
substantially weaker.
