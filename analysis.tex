\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}
In the main text, we retain only analyses that directly support our core
claims, namely how SPRM constructs reliable local credit from outcome-only
trajectories and how the global prior complements that local signal.
Implementation-focused analyses, including Stage 1 model selection, input
feature selection, IG baseline selection, and extended neighborhood
diagnostics, are deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix}.

Table~\ref{tab:ablation_processbench} reorganizes the ablation into four
blocks: local credit construction, consistency gating, the global-prior
branch, and head mixing.

The local-credit rows first compare three ways of forming local credit.
Using the degenerate ordered value difference alone as supervision causes an
almost complete collapse, which directly supports the discussion in
\Cref{sec:method_local}: once step credit is reduced to a fixed prefix
difference, it mostly redistributes sparse outcome reward rather than providing
a robust trainable local signal.
Removing the \texttt{question\_end} representation also causes a clear drop,
confirming that local attribution should not start from the first reasoning
step alone.
The transition from the problem statement to the reasoning chain already
contains information about how subsequent steps should be interpreted, so
including \(x_{i,0}\) gives the local scorer a better boundary anchor.
Taken together, this ablation shows that the local branch needs both a continuous
credit signal and an explicit boundary anchor.
This is why the final method is built on IG-based attribution rather than
training directly on the degenerate ordered difference.

The consistency-gate rows then ask how to denoise that IG-based local signal.
For the LLM gate, labels are generated by DeepSeek-V3.2 (2025.12.01).
Both consistency mechanisms substantially improve over using raw IG signs
directly, which confirms that local credit needs an explicit reliability check
before it is used as supervision.
The two mechanisms now tie on average F1, but with a different trade-off:
LLM consistency achieves the strongest error rejection, whereas
\(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\)-based consistency preserves much stronger
correct-path acceptance.
This advantage in \textit{Cor} is consistent with how
\(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\) is constructed in
\Cref{eq:pairwise_weight,eq:pairwise_counterfactual}:
the direct causal term is retained, while the interaction correction assigns
extra weight to positively contributing step pairs and amplifies coherent
multi-step support.
The design goal of this term is precisely to capture step-step interactions
rather than score each step in isolation, so when a correct trajectory depends
on mutually reinforcing local transitions, the causal gate is more likely to
preserve that chain of reasoning.
We therefore adopt \(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\) in the final method: it is
more faithful to the intended notion of trajectory-specific causal influence,
while matching the LLM-based alternative on average F1.

\subsection{Ablation II: Global Prior and Head Complementarity}
The last two blocks of Table~\ref{tab:ablation_processbench} ask a different
question: what does the global branch add beyond local credit, and does that
gain come from semantic neighborhood structure rather than generic smoothing?

The global-prior rows show that the global branch is complementary rather than
substitutive.
``Global only'' is much weaker than ``Local'', which indicates that
cross-trajectory regularities alone cannot replace trajectory-specific credit.
At the same time, ``Local + global'' achieves the best average F1 by trading a
modest drop in \textit{Cor} for a much larger gain in \textit{Err}, exactly
the trade-off expected when global semantic priors suppress locally plausible
but globally unreliable patterns.
The random-neighbor control further shows that this gain does not come from
adding an arbitrary smoothing branch:
replacing semantic retrieval with random neighbors slightly hurts both
\textit{Cor} and overall F1 relative to ``Local'', whereas the semantic
global branch produces a much larger F1 gain.

The head-mixing rows then show that head complementarity is not an all-or-nothing effect.
A small amount of global mixing can improve correct-path acceptance, as seen
from the strong \textit{Cor} at
\(\lambda_{\mathrm{loc}}=0.75,\lambda_{\mathrm{glo}}=0.25\), while the balanced
setting
\(\lambda_{\mathrm{loc}}=0.50,\lambda_{\mathrm{glo}}=0.50\) recovers the best
overall F1 and strongest \textit{Err}.
By contrast, placing too much weight on the global head sharply weakens both
\textit{Err} and overall F1.
This trend is consistent with the intended role of the two heads:
the local branch provides trajectory-specific evidence, while the global branch
acts as a stabilizing prior rather than a replacement signal.

This interpretation is also supported by the auxiliary neighborhood diagnostic:
the trajectory-mean \(\delta^{\mathrm{glo}}\) achieves an AUC of 0.856 against
final reward, with Pearson correlations of 0.613 to trajectory reward and
0.510 to step labels, while random-neighbor and reward-shuffle baselines are
substantially weaker.

Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix}.
