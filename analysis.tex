\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}
In the main text, we retain only analyses that directly support our core
claims, namely how SPRM constructs reliable local credit from outcome-only
trajectories and how the global prior complements that local signal.
Implementation-focused analyses, including Stage 1 model selection, input
feature selection, IG baseline selection, and extended neighborhood
diagnostics, are deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix}.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{ablation_study}
Under fixed backbone, training-data scale, and optimization hyperparameters, we
perform targeted ablations over the key SPRM mechanisms.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering

\caption{Targeted Ablation I: local credit and consistency on ProcessBench.
Metrics are averaged over GSM8K, MATH, OLYMPIADBENCH, and OMNIMATH.
Panel A compares how the local credit signal is constructed.
Panel B fixes IG with \texttt{question\_end} and compares different
consistency mechanisms.}
\label{tab:ablation_local_consistency}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Variant} & \textbf{Avg Err} & \textbf{Avg Cor} & \textbf{Avg F1} \\
\midrule
\multicolumn{4}{@{}l}{\textbf{Panel A: Local credit construction}} \\
Ordered value difference \(\Delta v\) & 44.8 & 67.4 & \textbf{53.8} \\

\midrule

No consistency & 37.7 & 76.5 & 50.2 \\
LLM consistency (DeepSeek-V3.2) & \textbf{47.3} & 70.6 & \textbf{55.8} \\
\(R^{\mathrm{causal}}\) consistency & 42.7 & \textbf{79.0} & 55.4 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:ablation_local_consistency} reorganizes the ablation around two
questions: how to construct the local credit signal itself, and how to make
that signal reliable enough to supervise the final PRM.

Panel A first compares three ways of forming local credit.
Removing the \texttt{question\_end} representation causes a clear collapse,
confirming that local attribution should not start from the first reasoning
step alone.
The transition from the problem statement to the reasoning chain already
contains information about how subsequent steps should be interpreted, so
including \(x_{i,0}\) gives the local scorer a better boundary anchor.
The ordered value difference \(\Delta v\) is a useful deterministic reference,
but the final method is built on IG-based attribution because it yields a
smoother local signal that can be paired with an explicit consistency test
rather than used as a fixed prefix difference alone.

Panel B then asks how to denoise that IG-based local signal.
Both consistency mechanisms substantially improve over using raw IG signs
directly, which confirms that local credit needs an explicit reliability check
before it is used as supervision.
The LLM-based consistency signal yields the highest average F1, mainly through
stronger error rejection, while \(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\)-based consistency
preserves the best correct-path acceptance.
We adopt \(R_{i,t}^{\mathrm{causal}}\) in the final method because it is more
faithful to the quantity defined in \Cref{sec:method_local}: whether the
current step changes the predicted value of the particular trajectory in which
it appears.

\subsection{Global Prior and Head Complementarity}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation_global_complementarity} asks a different question:
what does the global branch add beyond local credit, and does that gain come
from semantic neighborhood structure rather than generic smoothing?

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\footnotesize
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{3.8pt}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.08}
\caption{Targeted Ablation II: global prior and head complementarity on
ProcessBench.
Metrics are averaged over GSM8K, MATH, OLYMPIADBENCH, and OMNIMATH.
``Local + random-neighbor'' replaces the semantic prior with a random-neighbor
variant under the same local pipeline.}
\label{tab:ablation_global_complementarity}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Variant} & \textbf{Avg Err} & \textbf{Avg Cor} & \textbf{Avg F1} \\
\midrule
Local full & 42.7 & \textbf{79.0} & 55.4 \\
Local + random-neighbor & 43.8 & 74.6 & 55.2 \\
Global only & 26.0 & 46.9 & 33.4 \\
Full SPRM & \textbf{51.2} & 73.3 & \textbf{60.0} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The table shows that the global branch is complementary rather than
substitutive.
``Global only'' is much weaker than ``Local full'', which indicates that
cross-trajectory regularities alone cannot replace trajectory-specific credit.
At the same time, ``Full SPRM'' achieves the best average F1 by trading a
modest
drop in \textit{Cor} for a much larger gain in \textit{Err}, exactly the
trade-off expected when global semantic priors suppress locally plausible but
globally unreliable patterns.

The random-neighbor control further shows that this gain does not come from
adding an arbitrary smoothing branch.
Replacing semantic retrieval with random neighbors slightly hurts both
\textit{Cor} and overall F1 relative to ``Local full'', whereas the semantic
global branch produces a much larger F1 gain.
This interpretation is also supported by the auxiliary neighborhood diagnostic:
the trajectory-mean \(\delta^{\mathrm{glo}}\) achieves an AUC of 0.856 against
final reward, with Pearson correlations of 0.613 to trajectory reward and
0.510 to step labels, while random-neighbor and reward-shuffle baselines are
substantially weaker.
Detailed distribution plots and case studies are deferred to
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix}.
