\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}
In the main text, we retain only analyses that directly support our core claims, namely the robustness gains from filtering and the temporal Shapley formulation under causal ordering. Implementation-focused analyses---including Stage 1 model selection, input feature selection, and IG baseline selection---are deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix}.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{ablation_study}
Under fixed backbone, training-data scale, and optimization hyperparameters, we perform additive ablations over the key SPRM modules.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/ablation_all_metrics.pdf}
\caption{Ablation results of SPRM across all reported metrics. The figure summarizes the contribution of each module under a unified training/evaluation protocol.}
\label{fig:ablation_all_metrics}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:ablation_all_metrics} reports four variants under a unified protocol: (1) IG-only supervision without \textit{Question\_end} at $\langle\texttt{ST\_END}\rangle$; (2) IG + \textit{Question\_end}; (3) IG + \textit{Question\_end} + $R^{\text{causal}}$ filtering; and (4) the full SPRM that further adds $\delta_{\text{global}}$.

Three observations are consistent across metrics. First, introducing \textit{Question\_end} improves boundary calibration, showing that explicit question-to-reasoning transition signals are useful for step-level supervision. Second, adding $R^{\text{causal}}$ further stabilizes training and improves the balance between \textit{Err} and \textit{Cor}, indicating that causal-consistency filtering suppresses noisy local attributions. Third, incorporating $\delta_{\text{global}}$ brings the strongest gains in error localization on difficult domains; although \textit{Cor} may slightly decrease in some settings, the overall F1 improves, yielding the best trade-off in the full model.

Overall, the ablation confirms that SPRM's performance gain does not come from a single component: local attribution, causal filtering, and global priors contribute complementary benefits.

\subsection{Filter Mechanism}
We further evaluate whether the filtering mechanism stabilizes step-level attribution signals, and report all metrics under the same evaluation protocol.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/filter_all_metrics.pdf}
\caption{Impact of the filter mechanism on all evaluation metrics. The plot reports performance differences between variants with and without filtering strategies.}
\label{fig:filter_all_metrics}
\end{figure*}

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:filter_all_metrics}, the filtering mechanism provides more robust gains across multiple metrics. LLM Consistency is especially helpful for first-error localization, while $R^{causal}$ is more stable for judging correct paths, with larger improvements on harder datasets (OLYMPIADBENCH and OMNIMATH).

This trend suggests that raw local attributions contain non-trivial noise that accumulates over long trajectories. LLM Consistency mainly improves sensitivity-oriented indicators by enforcing semantic agreement across neighboring reasoning states, whereas $R^{causal}$ contributes more to path-level stability by suppressing transient attribution spikes that are inconsistent with downstream outcomes. Their gains are therefore complementary rather than redundant.

Notably, improvements are larger on OLYMPIADBENCH and OMNIMATH, where reasoning chains are longer and error propagation is stronger. This supports our hypothesis that filtering primarily improves robustness under distributional and trajectory-length stress, rather than only boosting average-case performance.

\subsection{Shapley Value in Time-series Scenarios}
We analyze whether Shapley-style attribution can provide a reliable step-level signal for temporally ordered reasoning trajectories. Classical Shapley offers a principled fairness baseline by averaging each participant's marginal contribution over all permutations of an unordered player set \citep{shapley1953value}.

\begin{equation}
\label{shapley_function}
\phi_i(v)=\mathbb{E}_{\pi}\Big[v(\mathrm{Pre}_{\pi}(i)\cup\{i\})-v(\mathrm{Pre}_{\pi}(i))\Big]
\end{equation}

In multi-step reasoning, however, steps are temporally ordered and causally dependent rather than freely permutable. For a trajectory $\tau=(s_1,\dots,s_T)\in\mathcal{D}$ with unique order $(1\prec 2 \prec \dots \prec T)$, the permutation expectation collapses to an ordered marginal form:
\begin{equation}
    \phi_t^{\text{ord}} := v_t - v_{t-1}, \qquad v_t := v(\{1,2,\dots,t\}),\; v_0:=v(\emptyset)
\end{equation}

This ordered form gives an interpretable first-order attribution baseline: positive $\phi_t^{\text{ord}}$ indicates value gain and negative $\phi_t^{\text{ord}}$ indicates potential error contribution. However, as a final supervision signal it is still insufficient for complex reasoning: it depends strongly on value-model calibration \citep{guo2025deepseek}, emphasizes immediate gains while missing delayed effects, under-represents step interactions, and can vary across contexts for semantically similar operations. We therefore treat ordered Shapley attribution as a local prior and combine it with causal filtering and global distribution-level priors to obtain more stable step-level supervision.
