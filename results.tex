\section{Experiments}
\subsection{Experimental Settings}
\paragraph{Benchmarks}
We evaluate SPRM on two representative benchmarks for process reward modeling:
\textbf{ProcessBench} \citep{zheng2025processbench}
and \textbf{PRMBench} \citep{DBLP:prmbench}.
ProcessBench focuses on step-level error localization in multi-step mathematical
reasoning, while PRMBench evaluates trajectory quality from complementary
perspectives.
We compare SPRM against a range of open-source PRMs and LLM-as-a-Judge
baselines.
Results are reported in Table~\ref{tab:processbench_res}
and Table~\ref{tab:prmbench_types_en}.

\paragraph{Training Data}
We train SPRM on Math-Shepherd trajectories \citep{wang2024math}.
Crucially, although Math-Shepherd provides MC-derived step annotations, we do
not use those intermediate labels when training SPRM.
Instead, we retain only rollout trajectories and their final outcome rewards,
and derive step-level supervision automatically from the trajectories
themselves together with outcome signals.

\paragraph{Baselines}
We compare with representative publicly available PRMs that differ in how they
construct process-level supervision:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Human-annotated supervision}:
    Qwen2.5-Math-7B-PRM800K \citep{zhang-etal-2025-lessons} is fine-tuned on
    PRM800K with Qwen2.5-Math-7B-Instruct \citep{Qwen2.5-MathTechnicalReport},
    and ReasonEval-7B \citep{DBLP:ReasonEval} is trained on human annotations
    emphasizing step validity and redundancy.
    \item \textbf{MC-based supervision}:
    Math-Shepherd-7B \citep{wang2024math} is trained with MC-annotated data;
    RLHFlow-DeepSeek-8B and RLHFlow-Mistral-8B \citep{dong2024rlhf} use
    MC-style process supervision with a different training formulation.
    \item \textbf{Outcome-reward broadcasting}:
    OVM-Qwen3-4B \citep{ovm:conf/naacl/YuGW24} models process-label statistics
    by broadcasting final rewards.
    We reproduce this baseline using its open-source training framework with
    Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 \citep{qwen3technicalreport} as the backbone.
    \item \textbf{First-error boundary modeling}:
    PQM-DeepSeek-7B-zeta4 \citep{DBLP:conf/iclr/LiL25a} models first-error
    boundaries with a ranking loss.
    \item \textbf{Curated process supervision}:
    Skywork-PRM-7B \citep{skywork} integrates o1-style deliberate reasoning
    strategies and is trained from Qwen2.5-7B-Math-Instruct.
\end{itemize}

These baselines cover representative paradigms for process supervision under
different assumptions: explicit human annotation, Monte Carlo step labeling,
and labels derived from final outcomes.

dataset, process-label source, and the main modeling choice that distinguishes
each method.

\paragraph{Metrics}
We follow the official evaluation protocols.
For ProcessBench, \textit{Err} and \textit{Cor} measure error localization on
incorrect trajectories and acceptance of correct trajectories, while
\textit{F1} summarizes their trade-off.
For PRMBench, \textit{Simplicity}, \textit{Soundness}, and
\textit{Sensitivity} respectively test preference for concise reasoning,
logical and semantic validity, and responsiveness to degraded or perturbed
trajectories; we report both category scores and the official overall score.

\paragraph{Implementation Details}
Our framework is implemented with three corresponding components.
Unless otherwise stated, we use Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 as the backbone.

\textbf{Trajectory scorer training}
We randomly sample 220K trajectories from Math-Shepherd and use the backbone to
extract final-layer hidden states.
We train a single-layer BiLSTM with batch size 128 and learning rate
\(1\times10^{-4}\), with early stopping (patience 5).
The data are split into train/validation/test with a ratio of
\(0.8/0.1/0.1\).

\textbf{Step-level attribution construction}
We compute step-level attribution signals using Integrated Gradients with
64 integration steps.
The IG target is the trajectory scorer logit.
For each step, we additionally compute a causal ablation score by replacing the
step feature with a baseline vector and measuring the score change.
The global prior is estimated through cross-trajectory semantic nearest-neighbor
retrieval.
We use Faiss \citep{douze2024faiss} with an IVF4096\_HNSW32,Flat index for
approximate search.
From a search pool of 400 trajectories, we retrieve the top 50 neighbors and
compute similarity-weighted reward statistics using ReLU-based weights.

\textbf{PRM training}
The constructed step-level signals are used to train the final process reward
model.
We perform LoRA fine-tuning on the backbone with a maximum context length of
4096.
Training runs for 1 epoch with learning rate \(5\times10^{-5}\) and global
batch size 32.

\paragraph{ProcessBench mainly highlights more robust step discrimination.}
The clearest pattern on ProcessBench is that SPRM achieves the best average F1
among PRMs through more robust step discrimination, maintaining a stronger
trade-off between \textit{Err} and \textit{Cor} instead of over-optimizing only
one side.
It reaches 60.0 average F1, with especially clear gains on OLYMPIADBENCH and
OMNIMATH, where F1 rises to 53.2 and 53.8.
This directly matches the design goal of SPRM:
the local attribution head is intended to identify where a trajectory starts to
go wrong, while the global semantic prior suppresses superficially plausible
but semantically unreliable patterns that would otherwise distort step credit.
This interpretation is also reflected in the baseline comparison.
Compared with Qwen2.5-Math-7B-PRM800K, which is trained with curated human
process labels, SPRM gives up some \textit{Cor} on the harder sets but gains
substantially on \textit{Err}, yielding a better overall F1.
Compared with OVM-Qwen3-4B, which derives labels by broadcasting final rewards,
the gap is much larger on \textit{Err}, suggesting that outcome broadcasting
alone is insufficient for precise error localization.

\paragraph{PRMBench highlights cleaner reasoning preferences and stronger reaction to degraded steps.}
PRMBench highlights a different advantage of SPRM: it prefers cleaner
reasoning and reacts more sharply to trajectory degradation.
SPRM reaches 57.9 on \textit{Simplicity} and 73.2 on \textit{Sensitivity},
including 64.4 on NCL and 58.0 on PS, which indicates that it is less likely
to reward redundant steps and more likely to penalize harmful edits or weakened
reasoning.
These behaviors align closely with the design of SPRM.
The local attribution head makes the model sensitive to step-level failures,
while the global prior suppresses semantically repetitive but superficially
plausible patterns, leading to a PRM that is both sharper and more selective.
Compared with the baselines, the remaining gap on \textit{Soundness} suggests
that explicit human supervision still helps with fine-grained semantic
consistency, but the overall picture is that SPRM converts outcome-derived
signals into a reward model with stronger trajectory sensitivity and cleaner
reasoning preferences.
