\section{Experiments}
\subsection{Experimental Settings}
\paragraph{Benchmarks}
We evaluate SPRM on two representative benchmarks for process reward modeling:
\textbf{ProcessBench} \citep{zheng2025processbench}
and \textbf{PRMBench} \citep{DBLP:prmbench}.
ProcessBench focuses on step-level error localization in multi-step mathematical
reasoning, while PRMBench evaluates trajectory quality from complementary
perspectives.
We compare SPRM against a range of open-source PRMs and LLM-as-a-Judge
baselines.
Results are reported in Table~\ref{tab:processbench_res}
and Table~\ref{tab:prmbench_types_en}.
Our main conclusion below is restricted to baseline PRMs in a comparable
practical regime.
Much larger judges or backbones are included as reference points rather than
as like-for-like competitors.
Among the baselines, OVM-Qwen3-4B is the most controlled comparison because it
shares the Qwen3-4B backbone and also derives process supervision from final
outcomes.

\paragraph{Training Data}
We train SPRM on Math-Shepherd trajectories \citep{wang2024math}.
Crucially, although Math-Shepherd provides MC-derived step annotations, we do
not use those intermediate labels when training SPRM.
Instead, we retain only rollout trajectories and their final outcome rewards,
and derive step-level supervision automatically from the trajectories
themselves together with outcome signals.

\paragraph{Baselines}
We compare with representative publicly available PRMs spanning human-annotated
supervision (Qwen2.5-Math-7B-PRM800K and ReasonEval-7B), Monte Carlo labels
(Math-Shepherd-7B and RLHFlow), outcome-reward broadcasting
(OVM-Qwen3-4B), first-error boundary modeling
(PQM-DeepSeek-zeta4-7B), and curated process supervision
(Skywork-PRM-7B).

Table~\ref{tab:baseline_supervision_compare} provides a compact comparison of
whether each model relies on automatically constructed supervision and whether
it avoids external annotation or evaluation pipelines.

\paragraph{Metrics}
We follow the official evaluation protocols.
For ProcessBench, \textit{Err} and \textit{Cor} measure error localization on
incorrect trajectories and acceptance of correct trajectories, while
\textit{F1} summarizes their trade-off.
For PRMBench, \textit{Simplicity}, \textit{Soundness}, and
\textit{Sensitivity} respectively test preference for concise reasoning,
logical and semantic validity, and responsiveness to degraded or perturbed
trajectories; we report both category scores and the official overall score.

\paragraph{Implementation Details}
Our framework consists of a trajectory scorer, step-level attribution
construction, and PRM training, all built on Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 unless
otherwise stated.
We keep the main text to the details needed to interpret the results and defer
the full training, attribution, and retrieval configuration to
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_impl_details}, including data split, batch size,
learning rates, epoch count, IG integration steps, and Faiss retrieval
hyperparameters.

\paragraph{ProcessBench mainly highlights more robust step discrimination.}
The clearest pattern on ProcessBench is that SPRM achieves the best average F1
within the matched baseline-PRM comparison, maintaining a stronger trade-off
between \textit{Err} and \textit{Cor} instead of over-optimizing only one side.
It reaches 60.0 average F1, with especially clear gains on OLYMPIADBENCH and
OMNIMATH, where F1 rises to 53.2 and 53.8.
This interpretation is consistent with the ablations in
\Cref{tab:ablation_processbench,sec:analysis}, where combining local credit
with the global prior yields the strongest overall F1 and substantially
improves \textit{Err}.
Compared with Qwen2.5-Math-7B-PRM800K, which is trained with curated human
process labels, SPRM gives up some \textit{Cor} on the harder sets but gains
substantially on \textit{Err}, yielding a better overall F1.
Compared with OVM-Qwen3-4B, which derives labels by broadcasting final rewards,
SPRM improves average F1 by 24.5 points and the gap is especially large on
\textit{Err}, suggesting that outcome broadcasting alone is insufficient for
precise error localization in this matched same-backbone comparison.

\paragraph{PRMBench highlights cleaner reasoning preferences and stronger reaction to degraded steps.}
PRMBench highlights a different advantage of SPRM: it prefers cleaner
reasoning and reacts more sharply to trajectory degradation.
Within the baseline-PRM comparison, SPRM reaches 58.7 on \textit{Simplicity}
and 74.3 on \textit{Sensitivity}, including 64.9 on NCL and 60.0 on PS, which
indicates that it is less likely
to reward redundant steps and more likely to penalize harmful edits or weakened
reasoning.
This reading is again consistent with
\Cref{tab:ablation_processbench,sec:analysis}, where the global prior improves
selectivity beyond the local branch alone and balanced head mixing gives the
best aggregate behavior.
Compared with the baselines, the remaining gap on \textit{Soundness} suggests
that explicit human supervision still helps with fine-grained semantic
consistency, but the overall picture is that SPRM converts outcome-derived
signals into a reward model with stronger trajectory sensitivity and cleaner
reasoning preferences.

\Cref{fig:bon_prm_grid} reports the better of the \texttt{min} and
\texttt{mean} aggregations for each model, averaged over reranking runs built
from 10 fixed random-seed candidate samples.
Under this fixed protocol, SPRM transfers cleanly from benchmark evaluation to
inference-time reranking.
Across all six plotted datasets, SPRM becomes the strongest PRM once the search
budget is moderately large, and it is the best model on every dataset at the
largest \(k\) shown in the figure.
The gains are especially clear on \textsc{MMLU-STEM}, where SPRM reaches
88.0\% and outperforms the strongest baseline by 17.0 points, and remain
substantial on \textsc{CollegeMath}, \textsc{Gaokao2023en}, \textsc{Math500},
and \textsc{OlympiadBench}, with gains of 6.2, 3.1, 3.0, and 3.0 points,
respectively.

This pattern is consistent with the main evaluation results and with the
complementary roles of the two heads in \Cref{tab:ablation_processbench}.
As \(k\) grows, reranking becomes increasingly sensitive to whether the PRM can
separate subtly flawed trajectories from genuinely strong ones.
SPRM is useful here because the local head sharpens step-level discrimination,
while the global prior suppresses semantically repetitive but low-quality
reasoning paths.
The result is not merely a higher static PRM score, but a reward signal that
improves search quality more reliably as the candidate set expands.
